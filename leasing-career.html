The Role

What does a leasing consultant actually do?

A leasing consultant is the face of an apartment community. When a prospective resident calls, emails, or walks in the door, you are the first person they meet. You show units, answer questions, guide people through the application process, explain the lease, and help new residents move in smoothly.

But the job is much more than showing apartments. Every day you are simultaneously doing sales, customer service, legal compliance, and relationship management. The best leasing consultants understand that full occupancy is the goal — and that every interaction either gets the property closer or further away from that goal.

"Leasing consultants are the engine of every apartment community. Without someone consistently converting prospects into signed leases and keeping residents happy enough to renew, even the best-located building empties out. This role is foundational to the entire property management industry."

The different levels in apartment leasing

Leasing Consultant

Entry point. Show units, process applications, sign leases, assist move-ins. No license required. W-2 employee of the property management company.

$18–$24/hr + bonuses

Senior Leasing Consultant

Handles complex leases and high-value prospects. Mentors junior staff. More administrative responsibility and higher commission tier.

$22–$30/hr + higher commission

Leasing Manager

Oversees the full leasing team at a community. Sets occupancy strategy, manages marketing, and reports directly to the property manager.

$48,000–$65,000/yr

What You'll Need

Skills that make you an exceptional leasing consultant

Essential

Sales & Communication

You are selling the lifestyle, the community, and the apartment — all at once. Warm, confident, natural communication turns a tour into a signed lease.

Essential

Fair Housing Knowledge

Every interaction — the ad you write, the question you ask, the tour you give — must comply with Fair Housing law. Ignorance is not a defense.

Essential

Organization & Follow-Up

You will juggle multiple prospects at different stages simultaneously. Consistent, timely follow-up is the single biggest driver of lease conversion.

Essential

Customer Service Under Pressure

Prospects ask the same questions repeatedly. Residents get frustrated. Staying calm, helpful, and professional in every interaction is what builds a reputation.

Helpful

Property Management Software

Yardi, Entrata, and RealPage are the dominant platforms. Most companies train you on their system — but walking in knowing the basics sets you apart.

Helpful

Basic Lease & Legal Literacy

You don't need to be a lawyer — but you do need to explain the lease clearly to residents and know which questions to escalate to your manager.

The Money

What can apartment leasing realistically earn you?

LA compensation reflects the high cost of living. Base pay is competitive, bonuses are tied directly to your leasing performance, and on-site housing discounts can dramatically increase your total compensation value.

Leasing Consultant (Entry)
$18–$24/hr
Base pay + monthly lease bonuses. No license required. On-site housing discount available.
Senior Leasing Consultant
$22–$30/hr
Higher base + increased commission tier. Typically reached after 1–2 years.
Leasing Manager
$48K–$65K
Manages the full leasing team. Occupancy strategy and marketing responsibility added.
Property Manager
$60K–$85K
Runs the full community operation. The natural next step for top leasing managers.

* Income figures reflect the Los Angeles market. Figures vary by company, community size, and performance.

Your Roadmap

How to get started in apartment leasing

1

Complete this curriculum

Work through all 8 modules of this track. By the time you finish, you will understand fair housing law, the leasing process, the application and screening system, and what to expect in your first 90 days on the job.

2

Apply for entry-level leasing consultant roles

Target large apartment communities (100+ units) operated by institutional companies like Greystar, Essex, AvalonBay, and FPI Management. These companies have the most consistent hiring, the clearest training programs, and the best-defined promotion paths.

3

Start the NALP coursework in your first 3 months

The National Apartment Leasing Professional (NALP) credential requires 6 months of experience and a proctored exam. Start the coursework immediately — you can complete much of it while building your experience hours on the job.

4

Get comfortable with your company's software

Yardi, Entrata, and RealPage are the three dominant property management platforms. Your employer will train you on their system, but take the time to learn it deeply. Speed and accuracy in the system is a competitive advantage.

5

Pursue promotion to Senior Consultant or Leasing Manager

After 12–18 months, position yourself for the next step. Build your occupancy track record, earn the NALP, and communicate your promotion goals directly to your property manager. Companies promote from within constantly in this industry.

6

Consider your California real estate license as a long-term upgrade

After 2+ years in leasing and property management, the California real estate license opens doors to sales, investment, and brokerage. It's not a prerequisite for success in leasing — it's a tool for expanding your career ceiling later.

Honest Assessment

Is apartment leasing right for you?

You'll thrive if you...

  • Are naturally outgoing and enjoy meeting new people
  • Like a job where every day is different
  • Are motivated by performance-based bonuses
  • Can stay organized across many simultaneous prospects
  • Enjoy helping people find a home, not just closing a deal
  • Are comfortable with compliance and documentation
  • Want a clear, proven path to property management

You may struggle if you...

  • Prefer quiet, solitary work with no customer interaction
  • Get stressed by rejection or frequent "no" answers
  • Struggle to follow legal procedures consistently
  • Don't like weekend or evening availability requirements
  • Are not comfortable with detailed paperwork and documentation
  • Prefer variable, unpredictable work over consistent systems
