Lesson 1 of 4

The appliance repair mindset — model number first, everything else second

Appliance repair is the skill that separates maintenance techs who save property managers money from ones who just pass calls to contractors. A property manager who has to call an appliance service company pays $75–$150 before a single repair is done — plus parts and labor. A maintenance tech who diagnoses and fixes the same problem with a $20 part saves that money on every single call, on every appliance, in every unit.

The foundation of appliance repair is a simple habit: always get the model number before searching for anything. Every appliance has a model number label — inside the refrigerator door frame, on the back panel, under the range top, inside the dishwasher door edge, inside the washer door. Write it down or photograph it before walking back to your cart. A search for "oven not igniting" returns thousands of generic results. A search for "Whirlpool WFG525S0HS not igniting" returns the exact repair video for that exact oven — often filmed by someone who had the identical problem on the identical unit.

The six-step research system from Module 2 applies directly here: model number first, YouTube search with model and symptom, parts diagrams on RepairClinic.com or PartSelect.com, read the comments on the repair video, check the manufacturer's error code guide if applicable, and know when to stop and escalate. Most appliance repairs you will encounter as a maintenance tech are within this scope. The exceptions — sealed refrigerant systems, gas valve internals, and anything requiring EPA 608 — are always escalated to licensed technicians.

💡 Parts Sites That Every Maintenance Tech Should Know

RepairClinic.com and PartSelect.com carry parts for virtually every major appliance brand ever manufactured. Enter the model number and they show you an exploded diagram of the entire appliance — exactly how it is assembled, what every part is called, and the part number for ordering. Both sites also have repair help sections with symptom-based guidance. When you have a model number and a symptom, these sites tell you exactly what part to order and often show you a repair video specific to that part on that model.

Lesson 2 of 4

Refrigerators and ovens — the highest-volume kitchen appliance calls

Refrigerators and ovens generate more maintenance calls than any other kitchen appliances. Most of these calls are resolved by cleaning something, replacing an inexpensive part, or resetting a safety device — not by replacing the appliance or calling a specialist.

Refrigerator — the maintenance tech checklist

Not cooling or running constantly: The most commonly overlooked cause is dirty condenser coils. These coils — located under the unit behind a kickplate or on the back — release heat from the refrigeration system. When caked with dust and debris, they cannot release heat efficiently, forcing the compressor to run continuously and reducing cooling performance. Clean with a coil brush and vacuum. Takes 10 minutes. Do this before assuming any mechanical failure.

Door not sealing: Test the door gasket by closing the door on a dollar bill. If it slides out easily with no resistance, the gasket is failing and warm air is entering the unit constantly. Gasket replacement is model-specific — order by model number. Most gaskets press into a channel around the door frame or are held by screws under the door liner. Cost: $15–$40. A failing door gasket causes the compressor to run constantly, wastes energy, and shortens compressor life.

Ice maker not working: Check the ice maker arm — if it is in the raised (off) position, the ice maker is disabled. Check the water supply line shutoff valve behind the refrigerator. Check for a frozen water supply line (common in units where the refrigerator was moved and the line kinked). If the ice maker hums but does not cycle, the water inlet solenoid valve may be faulty — a $20–$40 part that controls water flow into the ice maker.

Water on the floor inside or underneath: A clogged defrost drain is the most common cause of water pooling inside a refrigerator. The defrost system melts frost off the evaporator coil, and the water drains through a small drain hole at the bottom of the freezer compartment. When this drain clogs with debris, water backs up and leaks. Clear the drain with warm water and a small flexible brush or pipe cleaner. The drain opening is usually at the back of the freezer floor.

Gas oven — the igniter is almost always the answer

The most common gas oven failure is an oven that clicks or glows but does not light the burner. In modern gas ovens, a glow-bar igniter replaces the standing pilot — it heats up when the oven is turned on and opens the gas safety valve when it reaches sufficient temperature. When the igniter weakens with age, it glows but never gets hot enough to open the valve. The symptom is clear: the igniter glows orange, the oven does not heat, and you can sometimes smell gas briefly as the valve attempts to open.

Oven igniter replacement does not involve disconnecting any gas lines. The igniter is connected by a plug — unplug the old one, plug in the new one. The igniter is held by one or two screws and is accessible after removing the oven floor panel. Order by model number. Cost: $20–$50. Time: 20–30 minutes. Calling a service technician for the same repair: $150–$300.

⚠️ Always Check for Gas Smell Before Any Oven Repair

Before working on any gas appliance, smell the area around the appliance. If you detect a sulfur or rotten egg smell, stop immediately — do not turn on any lights, do not use your phone inside, do not create any spark. Exit the unit, leave the door open, and call the gas company from outside. Gas smell means gas leak — it is always a stop-everything emergency before any repair work begins.

Electric oven not heating: Electric ovens have two heating elements — a bake element at the bottom and a broil element at the top. If an element is visibly damaged (blistered, cracked, has a burn hole), it is faulty. Test with a multimeter for continuity — no continuity means the element is open. Replacement is a straightforward swap: unscrew the element, disconnect the two wires, connect the new element. Cost: $20–$50. Time: 15 minutes.

Lesson 3 of 4

Dryers, washers, and dishwashers — the laundry and kitchen appliance calls that never stop

Dryer — vent first, parts second

Before diagnosing any dryer problem, check the dryer vent. A clogged dryer vent is the most common cause of poor dryer performance — and also the most dangerous maintenance item in any apartment building. Clogged dryer vents cause approximately 2,900 residential fires per year. Lint is highly flammable. When the vent is clogged, heat builds up inside the dryer, blows thermal fuses, damages heating elements, and eventually creates a fire risk.

Signs of a clogged dryer vent: dryer takes multiple cycles to dry a single load, dryer feels very hot to the touch during operation, the exterior vent flap does not open when the dryer is running. Clear the vent by disconnecting the duct at the dryer, vacuuming from both ends, and confirming the exterior vent cap opens freely. Do this before replacing any parts — a clogged vent will blow a new thermal fuse within days from the same overheating that failed the original.

Dryer runs but does not heat: After confirming the vent is clear, the thermal fuse is the most likely culprit. The thermal fuse is a one-time safety device that blows permanently when the dryer overheats. Test with a multimeter for continuity — a blown fuse reads infinite (open circuit). Cost: $8–$15. Located near the exhaust duct connection inside the dryer cabinet. If the thermal fuse is good but the dryer still does not heat, the heating element itself may be open — test the same way.

Dryer runs but drum does not spin: The most common cause is a broken drive belt — the belt that wraps around the drum and the motor pulley. Open the dryer cabinet and check whether the drum turns freely by hand with no resistance. If it spins very easily (no tension), the belt is broken. Dryer belt replacement requires opening the cabinet and routing the new belt around the drum and pulleys — a 45-minute job with a model-specific YouTube video as your guide.

Washing machine — the lid switch is usually the answer

Top-loading washing machines have a lid switch — a safety device that prevents the machine from spinning with the lid open. When the lid switch fails, the machine fills and agitates normally but will not spin or advance to the drain cycle. To test: open the lid and press the lid switch plunger with a pen or screwdriver while the machine is in spin cycle. If the machine spins, the lid switch is the problem. Lid switch replacement costs $10–$25 and takes 20 minutes.

For washing machines that do not agitate, the agitator dogs (small plastic ratchet components inside the agitator) are often worn. These allow the agitator to move in one direction only — when worn, the agitator spins freely in both directions instead of ratcheting. Agitator dog replacement costs $5–$15 and is one of the fastest appliance repairs available — the agitator pulls straight up off the shaft, the dogs sit in a basket at the top, and replacement takes under 10 minutes.

Dishwasher — clean the filter before calling anyone

Most modern dishwashers have a removable filter at the bottom of the tub — a cylindrical screen that catches food debris before it recirculates through the pump. Most residents never clean it. A clogged filter causes standing water in the tub, poor cleaning performance, and drain problems. Removing and cleaning the filter takes two minutes: twist counterclockwise to unlock, lift out, rinse under running water, replace. Do this before diagnosing any drain pump or motor issue.

If the dishwasher still does not drain after cleaning the filter, check the drain hose for kinks — the hose runs from the dishwasher to the drain connection under the sink or to the garbage disposal. If the garbage disposal was recently replaced, confirm the knockout plug was removed from the dishwasher drain inlet on the disposal — this is one of the most common post-installation failures and takes 60 seconds to fix with a screwdriver.

Laundry and dishwasher repairs — model number first, YouTube second

Washer, dryer, and dishwasher repairs vary enough by brand and model that the best resource is always a model-specific YouTube video rather than a generic tutorial. Get the model number, then search YouTube using this format: [Brand] [Model Number] [Symptom]. You will almost always find a repair video filmed on the exact unit you are working on. RepairClinic.com and PartSelect.com also have model-specific parts diagrams and repair guides for every major brand.

💡 The Tech Sheet — The Hidden Repair Manual Inside Every Appliance

Most major appliances — especially washers and dryers — have a hidden paper "tech sheet" tucked inside the console or behind a body panel. It contains the complete wiring diagram and instructions for putting the machine into Diagnostic Mode to read error codes. Find it before calling anyone. On many units it is folded and tucked behind the front kick panel, inside the lid, or behind the back panel. It is the fastest path to a definitive diagnosis on any complex appliance fault.

🧺 Dryers — common repairs and what to search

Runs but does not heat: Blown thermal fuse or broken heating element. Test both with a multimeter for continuity. Always clear the dryer vent before replacing either part or the new parts will fail from the same overheating. Search: "[brand] [model] thermal fuse replacement" or "[brand] [model] heating element replacement."

Drum does not spin / squeaking: Broken drive belt, worn idler pulley, or worn drum support rollers. Pop the top or front panels to inspect. A drum that turns with no resistance (no tension) has a broken belt. Search: "[brand] [model] drive belt replacement" or "[brand] [model] drum roller replacement."

🫧 Washers — common repairs and what to search

Fills but does not spin (top loader): Faulty lid switch — the machine thinks the lid is open. Test by pressing the switch manually during spin cycle. Search: "[brand] [model] lid switch replacement."

Does not drain: Clogged or failed drain pump. Coins, socks, and small items frequently jam the pump impeller. Usually accessible from the bottom or back panel. Search: "[brand] [model] drain pump replacement" or "[brand] [model] drain pump clog."

Agitator does not agitate (top loader): Worn agitator dogs — the plastic ratchet pieces inside the agitator top. Agitator pulls straight up, dogs replace in under 10 minutes. Search: "[brand] [model] agitator dogs replacement."

🍽️ Dishwashers — common repairs and what to search

Not draining / standing water: Clean the filter first (two minutes, no tools). Check drain hose for kinks. If disposal was just replaced, check the knockout plug. Search: "[brand] [model] drain pump replacement" only after filter and hose are confirmed clear.

Not filling with water: Water inlet valve solenoid has likely failed. Located behind the bottom kickplate. Search: "[brand] [model] water inlet valve replacement."

Poor cleaning performance: Clogged spray arms — food debris or hard water scaling plugging the holes. They usually pop off with a twist. Soak in vinegar, clear holes with a toothpick. Search: "[brand] [model] spray arm cleaning" or "[brand] [model] spray arm replacement."

Leaking from front door: Door gasket failure. Pull old gasket from channel, press new one in. Search: "[brand] [model] door gasket replacement."

When to stop and escalate

Not every appliance problem is a maintenance tech repair. Stop and escalate when: the repair requires handling refrigerant (EPA 608 required), the repair involves internal gas valve components, the part cost exceeds what a replacement appliance would cost in the property's budget, or three separate repairs on the same appliance have failed to resolve the problem. A maintenance tech who knows when to escalate is more valuable than one who keeps attempting repairs past the point of diminishing returns. Document what you tried, what you found, and why you are escalating — that information helps whoever handles it next.

📋 Document Every Appliance Repair

Every appliance repair should be documented in the work order with: unit number, appliance brand and model number, symptom, what you checked, what you found, what part you replaced (with part number and cost), and the result. This creates a maintenance history per unit that helps the next tech, helps property managers make replacement decisions, and demonstrates your professionalism to every supervisor who reads your work orders.

The wrong thermal fuse was installed — a higher-rated fuse should have been used.

The dryer vent was not cleared — a clogged vent causes overheating that blows the thermal fuse repeatedly. The vent must be cleaned before or alongside any thermal fuse replacement.

The heating element is also failing and needs to be replaced alongside the thermal fuse.
D
The dryer is at end of life and should be replaced — repeated thermal fuse failures indicate general failure.
Correct! A thermal fuse that blows repeatedly is almost always caused by a clogged dryer vent that was not cleared before the replacement. The thermal fuse is a safety device that blows when the dryer overheats — if the vent is clogged, the dryer will continue to overheat, and any replacement fuse will blow within weeks for the same reason. Not correct. A thermal fuse that blows repeatedly is the signature of a clogged dryer vent that was not cleared. Run a diagnostic cycle — most modern washing machines have a self-test mode that identifies lid switch failures.

C

Correct! The lid switch pen test is the definitive no-parts diagnosis for a top-loader that fills but does not spin. The lid switch prevents the machine from spinning with the lid open — when it fails, the machine behaves as if the lid is always open, stopping after the fill cycle. By manually depressing the switch plunger while the machine is trying to spin, you bypass the failed switch. If the machine spins with the plunger pressed, the switch is confirmed faulty. A clogged filter causes standing water, poor cleaning performance, and drain problems. To clean: twist counterclockwise, lift out, rinse under water, replace. Takes two minutes and resolves the majority of dishwasher drain complaints.
💡 Always clean the dishwasher filter before diagnosing any drain pump or motor issue.
Door GasketRefrigerator Component
The flexible rubber seal around the refrigerator or freezer door that creates an airtight seal when closed. Test: close the door on a dollar bill — if the bill slides out with little resistance, the gasket is failing and warm air is entering the unit. A failing gasket forces the compressor to run constantly. Replace by model number — gaskets press into a channel or are held by screws under the door liner.
Dryer VentLife-Safety Maintenance Item
The exhaust duct that carries hot, lint-laden air from the dryer to the outside of the building. Clogged dryer vents are a leading cause of residential fires — approximately 2,900 per year nationally. Signs of clogging: dryer takes multiple cycles to dry clothes, dryer runs very hot, exterior vent flap does not open. Clean at least annually by disconnecting the duct and vacuuming from both ends. Always clear the vent before replacing any dryer parts.
Knockout PlugGarbage Disposal Installation
A plastic plug inside the dishwasher drain inlet port of every new garbage disposal. Must be removed with a screwdriver and hammer if the dishwasher drain hose connects to that port. Forgetting to remove it completely blocks the dishwasher drain — the most common cause of a dishwasher that stops draining immediately after a disposal is installed or replaced. Fix: knock it out from inside the disposal, remove the pieces before running the unit.
Lid SwitchWasher Safety Component
A safety device on top-loading washing machines that prevents the machine from spinning with the lid open. When it fails, the machine fills and may agitate but will not advance to the spin or drain cycle — it behaves as if the lid is always open. Test: manually press and hold the switch plunger with a pen while the machine is in spin cycle. If it spins, the switch is faulty. Replacement cost: $10–$25, approximately 20 minutes.
Oven IgniterGlow-Bar IgniterGas Oven Component
The component in a modern gas oven that replaces the standing pilot light. When the oven is turned on, the igniter heats up and opens the gas safety valve when it reaches sufficient temperature — typically 1,000–1,100°F. A weak igniter glows orange but does not reach the threshold temperature, so the valve never opens and the oven never heats. Replacement is a plug-in swap with no gas line disconnection. Cost: $20–$50. Most common gas oven failure.
Thermal FuseDryer Safety Component
A one-time safety device in electric dryers that permanently opens (blows) if the dryer overheats, cutting power to the heating element. Most common cause of a dryer that runs but does not heat. Test with multimeter for continuity — good fuse reads near zero ohms, blown fuse reads infinite. Cost: $8–$15. Always clear the dryer vent before replacing — a clogged vent will blow the new fuse immediately from the same overheating condition.
