Lesson 1 of 4

The essential tools — what to buy first and why

You do not need thousands of dollars in tools to start your maintenance career. What you need is the right tools — the ones that cover 75–80% of every work order you will ever receive. As an experienced maintenance supervisor who trains new techs puts it: having your own tools signals that you are serious about learning. A supervisor choosing between two new hires will almost always invest more time training the one who shows up with their own bag — because that person has already demonstrated they are committed.

Start with quality where it counts — channel locks, screwdrivers, adjustable wrenches — and go budget where it does not. Harbor Freight and Home Depot have perfectly serviceable versions of many tools that will hold up fine through your first year. You can upgrade as you earn. The goal on day one is to have what you need to learn, not to have the best collection in the shop.

💡 What Your First Tool Kit Actually Costs

A fully functional beginner maintenance tool kit covering all the basics runs approximately $160–$500 total depending on brand choices. Pepe Gomez built a fully capable tote for $160–$170 using mostly Home Depot Husky and Stanley brand tools. A more comprehensive kit including power tools runs closer to $400–$500. You do not have to buy everything at once — start with the hand tools and add power tools as you earn your first paychecks.

Your core tool kit — organized by category

🔧 Hand Tools — The Foundation

  • Channel locks — 2 sizes (10" and 6"). Channel Lock brand lasts forever
  • Multi-bit screwdriver (6-in-1 or 11-in-1) — quarter inch and 5/16 nut drivers included
  • Dedicated Phillips #2 and flat head screwdrivers
  • Needle nose pliers — standard and long-reach (for sink drains)
  • Adjustable wrenches — 6" and 8"
  • Vice grips — standard and needle nose
  • Hammer — nothing fancy, harbor freight is fine
  • Utility knife — you will use this every single day
  • Allen wrench set — metric and standard
  • Hacksaw — for cutting pipe and conduit
Budget: $60–$100 for this full category

🔌 Electrical Tools

  • Non-contact voltage tester — tells you if something is live before you touch it
  • GFCI outlet tester — plug in, instantly diagnoses outlet wiring issues
  • Wire strippers / dikes — Klein brand recommended
  • Linesman's pliers — for twisting wire and heavy-duty gripping
  • Multimeter — get one that reads microfarads for capacitor testing later
  • Electrical tape — multiple colors as you advance
The non-contact tester and GFCI tester are must-haves from day one — they keep you safe

🚿 Plumbing Tools

  • Faucet removal tool ("easy faucet remover") — $20, saves hours under sinks
  • Plunger — toilet and sink styles
  • Toilet auger / closet snake
  • Teflon tape — every plumbing job needs this
  • Pick set — for O-rings and small components
  • Caulk gun — ratcheting style preferred
The faucet removal tool is one of the highest-value $20 investments in your entire kit

📐 Measurement & Layout

  • Tape measure — 25 foot Stanley Power Lock
  • Torpedo level — for hanging things, checking doors
  • Stud finder — basic model is fine for most properties
  • Speed square — marking straight lines for cuts
  • Notepad and pen — write down apartment numbers, parts needed, observations
A notepad is underrated — when you are running 10+ work orders a day, apartment numbers blur together

💡 Lighting

  • Work light — battery powered, for under sinks and dark spaces
  • Flashlight or headlamp — residents keep their apartments dark, always have light
You will be amazed how dark some apartments are — do not get caught without light

⚡ Power Tools

  • Cordless drill/driver — 12V is fine for apartment work
  • Impact driver — for driving screws fast
  • Drill bits — wood and metal, keep cheap spares
  • Driver bits — Phillips, flat, Torx, square
Stick to one battery platform (Milwaukee, DeWalt, Ryobi) — one set of batteries charges everything
🌡️ Pro Tip — The Thermometer

One tool most beginners miss but experienced techs swear by: a digital thermometer. When a resident says their AC is blowing hot or their water is not hot enough, you can stick the thermometer in the vent or run the tap and get an actual reading — 59°F at the AC vent, 118°F at the hot tap. Write that number on your work order. Now instead of your word against the resident's, you have documented data. It ends arguments instantly and protects you from false complaints. A basic digital thermometer costs $15–$30.

Lesson 2 of 4

Safety on the job — protecting yourself and the people around you

Maintenance work involves real physical hazards — electricity, plumbing under pressure, sharp tools, heavy objects, ladders, and occasionally mold or chemical exposure. Most accidents are preventable with basic habits and the right protective gear. Building safety habits from day one is not about being overly cautious — it is about staying in the game long enough to build a career.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) — what to wear and when

Safety Glasses

Wear any time you are cutting, drilling, grinding, or working under a sink where debris can fall in your eyes. Keep a pair in your bag at all times.

Work Gloves

Cut-resistant gloves for sharp edges and broken glass. Nitrile disposable gloves for plumbing work — protect from bacteria and chemicals.

Steel-Toe Boots

Non-negotiable. You will be carrying heavy appliances, climbing ladders, and working in tight spaces all day. Protect your feet.

Dust Mask / N95

For drywall sanding, insulation work, attic access, or any work that kicks up dust or particles. Keep a few in your tool bag.

Knee Pads

You will spend a lot of time on hard floors working under sinks and on toilets. Knee pads are a long-term investment in your body.

Voltage Tester

Always test before touching any wire or outlet. The non-contact voltage tester is your first line of defense against electrical shock.

Core safety habits — do these every time

Turn off the water before any plumbing repair. Find the shutoff valve under the sink, behind the toilet, or at the main shutoff and turn it off before disconnecting any supply line or fitting. A supply line blowing off under full pressure will flood an apartment in minutes.

Test before you touch. Before working on any outlet, switch, or light fixture — use your non-contact voltage tester. Even if you turned off the breaker, test again. Breakers are mislabeled. Circuits are shared. Always test.

Never work alone on a ladder in an occupied unit. If you need to work at height — ceiling fans, high fixtures, attic access — let someone know where you are. A fall with no one nearby is a serious emergency.

When in doubt — stop and call. If you start a repair and discover something unexpected — wiring that does not look right, a gas smell, water damage behind a wall, suspected mold — stop the repair, document what you found, and report it to your supervisor immediately. This is not weakness. This is professional judgment.

⚠️ Gas Smells — Always Stop Immediately

If you smell gas in a unit — do not turn any lights on or off, do not use your phone inside the unit, do not create any spark of any kind. Exit the unit immediately, leave the door open, evacuate nearby units if the smell is strong, and call the gas company from outside. Gas leaks are not a maintenance repair — they are an emergency. This is one of the items on the "always call a pro" list that has zero exceptions.

When to handle it yourself — and when to call a licensed professional

One of the most valuable skills a maintenance tech can develop is knowing exactly where their authority ends. Attempting work that requires a license — gas lines, main electrical panel work, structural repairs — creates real liability for you and the property owner. It can get you fired, fined, or worse. The best maintenance techs are not the ones who try to fix everything — they are the ones who know exactly what they can handle, handle it well, and escalate everything else quickly and clearly.

💡 The High-Value Swap Mindset

Some of the most profitable repairs a maintenance tech can do are simple part swaps that look intimidating from the outside. An oven igniter replacement takes 20 minutes and costs $20–$50 in parts — a service call for the same repair runs $150–$300. A refrigerator door gasket replacement takes 10 minutes and costs $20 — same story. A toilet fill valve swap takes 15 minutes and costs $12. When you know which repairs are simple part swaps — and you have the tools and confidence to do them — you become enormously valuable to any property manager who would otherwise have to call a specialist every time.

How to research any unfamiliar repair — the system that makes you never get stuck

Every maintenance tech — no matter how experienced — runs into unfamiliar repairs. A model of appliance they have never seen. A system configuration they have not worked on before. A symptom they cannot immediately diagnose. The best techs are not the ones who know everything — they are the ones who know how to find the answer fast. Here is the system that works.

1

Get the model number first — before searching anything

Every appliance has a model number — inside the refrigerator door frame, on the back of the unit, under the range, inside the dishwasher door. Write it down. A generic search for "dishwasher not draining" returns generic results. A search for "Whirlpool WDT730PAHZ not draining" returns the exact repair for that exact machine — often with a video of someone fixing the identical issue on the identical model.

2

Search YouTube with the model number and the symptom

YouTube is the single most powerful training resource available to any maintenance tech. Search "[brand] [model number] [symptom]" — "Rheem water heater pilot won't stay lit," "GE refrigerator not cooling model GSS25GGH," "Carrier AC not cooling." There is almost always a video of a real technician fixing the exact same problem on the exact same unit. Watch the full video before touching anything. As Pepe Gomez says: "The best trade school ever — YouTube."

3

Check parts sites for exploded diagrams

RepairClinic.com, PartSelect.com, and AppliancePartsPros.com have exploded diagrams for virtually every appliance ever manufactured. You can see exactly how the unit is assembled before you start disassembling it. These sites also sell the parts — once you identify what you need, you can confirm the part number and order it or find it locally.

4

Read the YouTube comments

This sounds minor but it is genuinely valuable. Other people who have done the same repair on the same unit leave comments about what went wrong, what tools they needed that the video did not mention, what the actual gotchas were. "Warning — the clip on the left side is plastic and snaps if you are not careful" is the kind of tip that saves you from making a bad repair worse.

5

Check the manufacturer's website and error codes

Most major appliance brands (GE, Whirlpool, Samsung, LG, Rheem, Carrier) publish repair documentation and error code guides on their websites or YouTube channels. Error codes especially — a flashing light or digital error code on a modern appliance almost always has a specific meaning that points you directly to the problem. Do not start diagnosing until you have looked up the error code.

6

Know when to stop

If your research reveals the repair requires specialized tools you do not have, involves a gas line, requires handling refrigerant (EPA 608 required), or the part costs more than a replacement unit would — stop. Document what you found, report it to your supervisor, and let the right person handle it. Knowing your limits is not a weakness. It is exactly what property managers want from a professional maintenance tech.

📋 Document Everything — Always

Every repair you complete should be documented in your work order with more than just "fixed." Write what you found, what you did, what parts you used, and any observations you made during the Unit Scan. Good documentation protects you from false complaints, helps the next tech who works on that unit, gives your property manager the data they need to budget for future repairs, and builds your professional reputation as a thorough, reliable technician. A few extra sentences on a work order costs 60 seconds and is worth far more than that.

📖 Module 2 — Key Terms & Definitions

All terms introduced in this module. Search to find any definition instantly.

Channel LocksHand Tool
Adjustable pliers with a slip-joint mechanism allowing the jaw to be set at multiple widths. One of the most-used tools in apartment maintenance — for gripping pipes, fixtures, supply lines, and hundreds of other applications. Get at least two sizes: 10" and 6". Channel Lock brand is recommended for quality at a reasonable price.
Error CodeAppliance Diagnostics
A specific code displayed by a modern appliance (as a number, letter combination, or flashing light pattern) that indicates a specific fault or problem. Always look up error codes before diagnosing — they point directly to the problem and save significant troubleshooting time. Find error code guides on the manufacturer's website or YouTube.
💡 Never start diagnosing a modern appliance without first checking if it is displaying an error code.
GFCI Outlet TesterGround Fault Circuit InterrupterElectrical Safety Tool
A plug-in diagnostic tool that tests whether an outlet is wired correctly, has power, and whether the GFCI protection is functioning. Essential for diagnosing "outlets not working" work orders. Also has a test button to trip GFCI outlets. Costs approximately $10–$15 and is one of the highest-value tools in any maintenance tech's bag.
High-Value SwapProfessional Concept
A repair that appears complex or specialized from the outside but is actually a simple part replacement requiring no license and minimal tools. Examples: oven igniter ($20–$50 part, 20 minutes vs. $150–$300 service call), refrigerator door gasket ($20, 10 minutes), dryer heating element ($30, 30 minutes). Knowing which repairs are high-value swaps is a core competency that makes a maintenance tech extremely valuable to property managers.
Model NumberRepair Research
A unique identifier for a specific appliance or equipment unit, usually found on a label inside the door, on the back panel, or underneath the unit. The most important piece of information to collect before researching any unfamiliar repair. A model-specific YouTube search returns exact repair videos for that exact unit — far more useful than generic searches.
MultimeterElectrical Diagnostic Tool
A handheld electronic measuring instrument used to test voltage, continuity, resistance (ohms), and — in models that support it — capacitance (microfarads). Essential for diagnosing electrical faults and testing capacitors on HVAC systems. Get one that reads microfarads — it unlocks HVAC diagnostic work which is the highest-paid skill set in apartment maintenance.
💡 If you have a multimeter and want to learn HVAC diagnostics, supervisors are much more likely to teach you — they can see you are serious.
Non-Contact Voltage TesterElectrical Safety Tool · Must-Have
A pen-shaped tool that detects the presence of electrical voltage without touching the wire — just hold it near the wire and it beeps or lights up if the circuit is live. Must be used before touching any wire or electrical component, even after turning off a breaker. Breakers are frequently mislabeled in older buildings. Always test. Cost: approximately $15–$25.
PPEPersonal Protective Equipment
Protective gear worn to reduce exposure to workplace hazards. For maintenance techs: safety glasses (drilling, cutting, under-sink work), work gloves (cut-resistant and nitrile disposable), steel-toe boots (daily), dust mask/N95 (drywall, insulation, attic work), knee pads (floor-level plumbing work). PPE should be standard practice, not an afterthought.
Teflon TapeThread Seal Tape / PTFE TapePlumbing Supply
A thin white tape wrapped around threaded pipe fittings before assembly to create a watertight seal. Required on virtually every plumbing repair involving threaded connections — supply line connections, shower arms, toilet supply lines, and more. Inexpensive and essential. Wrap clockwise (same direction as tightening) for proper sealing.
Thermometer (Digital)Diagnostic Tool · Documentation
A digital temperature measuring device used to document actual temperatures rather than estimates. For maintenance techs: used to measure AC vent output temperature, hot water temperature at the tap, and other temperature-dependent diagnostics. Eliminates "your word vs. the resident's word" disputes by producing objective, documentable data. Cost: $15–$30. One of the most underrated tools in apartment maintenance.
