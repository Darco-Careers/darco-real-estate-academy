Lesson 1 of 4

The six steps of mortgage loan origination — pre-qualification through servicing

The mortgage loan process follows a defined sequence that every MLO must know in order — both because it governs your daily work and because the NMLS exam tests it directly. The six steps are pre-qualification, application, processing, underwriting, closing, and servicing. Each stage has specific actions, disclosures, and legal requirements attached to it.

1

Pre-Qualification

The borrower gets an initial estimate of how much they can borrow based on self-reported income, assets, and credit. This is a preliminary assessment — not a commitment. Important distinction for the exam: a mortgage broker can only pre-qualify (estimate based on information provided). A mortgage lender can issue a pre-approval (based on documented, verified information). Pre-qualification gives the borrower a realistic budget range before they begin home shopping.

2

Application — The 1003 Form

The borrower formally applies for the loan. The standard residential loan application is called the Uniform Residential Loan Application — Form 1003 (pronounced "ten-oh-three"), created by Fannie Mae (FNMA — Federal National Mortgage Association). The 1003 captures: borrower name and Social Security number, address history (2 years), employment history (2 years), income, assets, credit, property details, and loan purpose. Critical exam point: a lender does not need a completed 1003 to trigger federal disclosure obligations. Once six key pieces of information are collected — name, income, SSN, property address, estimated property value, and loan amount sought — the three-business-day clock for the Loan Estimate begins, regardless of whether the application is on a formal document or a napkin.

3

Processing

The loan processor collects and verifies all documentation needed for underwriting. The file must be complete and accurate before it moves forward. The processor also orders the appraisal, title search, and title insurance, and may send out verifications of employment (VOEs). The loan officer may handle processing themselves or hand off to a dedicated processing department.

4

Underwriting

The underwriter evaluates risk and issues an approval, conditional approval, suspension (needs more information), or denial. Most files are first run through an Automated Underwriting System (AUS) — Fannie Mae's system is Desktop Underwriter (DU), Freddie Mac's is Loan Prospector (LP). If the AUS approves the loan, a human underwriter then verifies that all documented information matches the application. If denied, the lender must provide an adverse action notice within 30 days under ECOA and the Fair Credit Reporting Act.

5

Closing

The loan becomes official. The borrower signs the closing disclosure and loan documents, funds are dispersed, and ownership transfers. The closing is also called the day of consummation — the point at which the borrower is legally obligated to the loan. The Closing Disclosure (CD) must be provided at least 3 business days before closing under TRID. For refinances (not purchases), borrowers have a 3-business-day right of rescission under TILA to cancel the transaction.

6

Servicing

After closing, the loan enters servicing — the ongoing management of monthly payments, escrow accounts, property tax and insurance payments, and default/foreclosure procedures if payments lapse. Loan servicing is regulated by RESPA. Servicing rights are often sold separately from the loan itself — borrowers must receive notice at least 15 days before a servicing transfer. During the first 60 days after a transfer, borrowers cannot be penalized for sending payments to the old servicer.

Lesson 2 of 4

The five C's of creditworthiness — how underwriters evaluate risk

When an underwriter evaluates a loan file, they are fundamentally answering one question: will this borrower repay this loan? The framework for that evaluation is the five C's of creditworthiness — a structured way to assess risk across five dimensions. Four of the C's form the core underwriting standard. The fifth — conditions — adds market and situational context. The NMLS exam tests your ability to identify and distinguish all five.

C₁

Credit (Character)

The borrower's history of repaying debts — reflected in FICO scores and credit report payment history. Credit reveals willingness to pay. FICO scores are generated by the Fair Isaac Corporation. Lenders pull all three bureaus (TransUnion, Experian, Equifax) and use the middle score of the three for each borrower.

C₂

Capacity

The borrower's ability to repay — measured by the debt-to-income (DTI) ratio. Front-end DTI = housing expenses ÷ gross monthly income. Back-end DTI = all monthly debts ÷ gross monthly income. This is the most complex C and the most common source of errors by inexperienced loan officers. Income calculation is fixed — errors here can kill a file with no workaround.

C₃

Capital

The borrower's savings, assets, and reserves — the down payment, closing costs, and months of reserves available after closing. Underwriters review 2 months of bank statements. Large unexplained deposits must be sourced and documented. Gift funds require a gift letter. Seasoning means funds have been in the account for 2+ statement cycles.

C₄

Collateral

The property securing the loan — its appraised value, condition, and title status. The appraisal confirms value and flags property condition issues. The title report identifies any clouds on title — liens, easements, or unresolved claims — that must be cleared before closing. An "as is" appraisal means no repairs required; "subject to" means repairs must be completed before funding.

C₅

Conditions

The broader context surrounding the loan — market conditions, job stability, the purpose of the loan, current interest rates, and local economic factors. Conditions also refers to the specific items on a conditional approval letter that must be cleared before the loan can fund. Conditions are not a denial — they are a list of requirements the borrower and loan officer must satisfy to receive final approval and a "clear to close."

💡 Capacity is the Most Dangerous C

Income calculation is where the most experienced-based errors occur. The reason: income calculation is a fixed historical fact — once the underwriter determines the correct number, there is almost nothing the borrower or loan officer can do to change it. A self-employed borrower's net income after deductions, variable income that requires a 2-year average, a second job that needs a 2-year history, an employment gap that needs to be explained — all of these are income calculation complexities that can collapse a pre-approval if the loan officer did not calculate them correctly from the start. Always verify complex income scenarios before promising a borrower they are approved.

Lesson 3 of 4

TRID disclosures — the Loan Estimate and the Closing Disclosure

The TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure rule — known as TRID — was implemented by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) in 2015. TRID combined the disclosure requirements of the Truth in Lending Act (TILA) and the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA) into two standardized forms: the Loan Estimate and the Closing Disclosure. These two forms represent some of the most heavily tested content on the NMLS exam.

📋 Loan Estimate (LE)

  • Replaces the old Good Faith Estimate (GFE) and early TILA disclosure
  • Must be provided within 3 business days of receiving the application
  • Triggered by receipt of 6 key application data points — not a completed 1003
  • Shows estimated interest rate, monthly payment, and closing costs
  • Allows borrowers to compare loan offers side-by-side
  • Cannot be charged fees (except credit report fee) until borrower acknowledges receipt and indicates intent to proceed
  • Certain costs cannot change at closing; others can increase within limits

📋 Closing Disclosure (CD)

  • Replaces the old HUD-1 Settlement Statement
  • Must be provided at least 3 business days before the scheduled closing
  • Shows final, actual loan terms and costs
  • Borrower can compare CD to LE to verify no unexpected changes
  • If certain key terms change, a new 3-business-day waiting period is required
  • Borrower signs the CD at the closing table
  • Day of consummation = the day the borrower becomes legally obligated to the loan

Documentation collected during processing

Recent pay stubs (last 30 days)
W-2 forms (last 2 years)
Federal tax returns (last 2 years)
Bank statements (last 2 months)
Purchase contract (signed by both parties)
Credit report (pulled by lender)
Proof of assets (investment accounts, 401k)
Photo ID and Social Security verification
Profit & loss statement (self-employed borrowers)
Gift letter (if using gift funds for down payment)

Lesson 4 of 4

Conditional approval, clear to close, and the right of rescission — the final stretch

Most loan approvals are not clean approvals — they are conditional approvals. This means the underwriter has approved the loan subject to a list of conditions that must be satisfied before the loan can fund. Understanding conditional approvals and how to clear conditions efficiently is one of the most practical skills a working MLO develops.

What happens after a conditional approval

1

Receive the conditional approval letter

The underwriter issues an approval with a list of conditions — typically 8 to 15 items. Some are borrower conditions (provide an updated pay stub, explain a large deposit, provide a gift letter). Others are lender or title conditions (update the mortgage clause on the homeowner's insurance, clear a title lien). Your job as the MLO is to identify which conditions require borrower action and communicate them clearly and quickly.

2

Clear conditions and resubmit

Gather the required documentation for each condition and submit back to the underwriter. Some conditions are black-and-white — easy to satisfy. Others fall into gray areas where the loan officer may need to advocate with the underwriter or escalate to an underwriting manager. The ability to argue gray-area conditions is where experienced MLOs separate themselves from beginners.

3

Receive the clear to close (CTC)

When all conditions are satisfied, the underwriter issues a clear to close — authorization to proceed to the closing table. The closing disclosure must then be issued at least 3 business days before the scheduled closing date. The closing date cannot move forward until those 3 days have elapsed from CD delivery.

4

Closing — day of consummation

The borrower meets with the settlement agent (title company or attorney depending on state). They review and sign the closing disclosure and all loan documents. Funds are dispersed — the seller receives sale proceeds, the borrower takes possession. The borrower should bring a cashier's check or wire transfer for the down payment and closing costs. Once signed and funded, the transaction is complete.

Right of Rescission — refinances only

Under the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), borrowers have a 3-business-day right of rescission on refinance transactions that use their primary residence as collateral. This applies to refinances, home equity loans, and HELOCs — it does not apply to purchase transactions. During the rescission period, the lender cannot disperse funds. If the borrower exercises their right to rescind, the lender must return all fees and charges paid. If the lender fails to provide two copies of the notice of right to rescind, the rescission period extends to 3 years.

⚠️ Ethics Alert — Closing with Changed Circumstances

The NMLS exam tests ethics scenarios at the closing stage. The most common: a borrower's employment situation changes between loan approval and closing — they lose their job, their income is reduced, or they take on significant new debt. An MLO has an ethical (and legal) obligation to update the lender with material changes in the borrower's financial situation. Closing a loan when you know the borrower no longer qualifies is a violation of lending laws and professional ethics. The right answer on every exam ethics question involving changed circumstances is always: disclose to the lender and do not close the loan until the situation is re-evaluated.

D

Every loan file is a story. Pre-qualification is the first page — we think we know where it ends. Application is the middle — the details start to emerge. Processing is where the real picture forms. Underwriting is where the truth comes out. Closing is the ending everyone has been working toward. And servicing is the relationship that continues for the next 30 years. Your job as an MLO is to be the author who keeps that story moving forward — solving problems, communicating clearly, and never losing sight of the family on the other end of the file who is waiting to get their keys.

📌 Module 3 Key Takeaways

  • The six steps of the mortgage loan process in order: pre-qualification → application → processing → underwriting → closing → servicing. The NMLS exam tests this sequence directly — know it cold.
  • The Uniform Residential Loan Application is Form 1003, created by Fannie Mae (FNMA). Federal disclosure is triggered when 6 key data points are collected — not when the 1003 is completed. The Loan Estimate must be delivered within 3 business days of application.
  • Brokers can only pre-qualify borrowers; lenders can issue pre-approvals. Pre-approval is stronger because it is based on verified documentation, not self-reported information.
  • The five C's of creditworthiness: Credit (willingness to pay — FICO scores), Capacity (ability to pay — DTI ratio), Capital (down payment, reserves, assets), Collateral (the property — appraisal and title), Conditions (market context and conditional approval items). All five are tested on the NMLS exam.
  • Automated Underwriting Systems: Desktop Underwriter (DU) belongs to Fannie Mae. Loan Prospector (LP) belongs to Freddie Mac. AUS approvals speed the process but still require human underwriter verification of all documented information.
  • TRID (2015) created two key forms: the Loan Estimate (provided within 3 business days of application) and the Closing Disclosure (provided at least 3 business days before closing). These replaced the Good Faith Estimate and HUD-1.
  • Right of rescission applies to refinances, HELOCs, and home equity loans — NOT purchase transactions. Borrowers have 3 business days to cancel. If the lender fails to provide two copies of the rescission notice, the period extends to 3 years.

🧠 Knowledge Check

5 questions — click your answer, then check all at once.

1. A loan officer is speaking with a potential borrower over the phone. During the conversation, the borrower provides their name, Social Security number, income, the property address they want to purchase, an estimated value of the property, and the loan amount they are seeking. The loan officer has not had the borrower complete the 1003 form yet. Has a loan application been triggered under TRID — and what does that mean?

A
Yes — a loan application has been triggered. Under TRID, receipt of six specific pieces of information — name, income, SSN, property address, estimated property value, and loan amount sought — constitutes an application regardless of whether a formal 1003 has been completed. This starts the 3-business-day clock for providing the Loan Estimate.
B
No — an application is only triggered when the borrower signs and submits the completed Uniform Residential Loan Application (Form 1003). A phone conversation does not constitute a formal application under TRID.
C
No — the loan estimate clock only starts when the loan officer pulls the borrower's credit report, which is required before any application can be considered received.
D
Yes — but the 3-business-day clock for the Loan Estimate only begins when the borrower signs an intent to proceed, not when the six data points are collected.

2. What is the key difference between Fannie Mae's Desktop Underwriter (DU) and the role of the human underwriter — and why does an AUS approval not eliminate the need for a human underwriter?

A
DU and the human underwriter perform identical functions — when DU issues an "Approve/Eligible" finding, the human underwriter simply stamps the file approved without conducting any additional review.
B
DU evaluates the loan application data against Fannie Mae's risk guidelines and issues a finding (Approve/Eligible, Refer, or Ineligible) within seconds. However, DU cannot verify that the documented information actually matches what was entered. The human underwriter then reviews all documentation (pay stubs, bank statements, appraisal, title) to confirm everything lines up with the AUS finding. An AUS approval is a conditional pathway — it still requires human verification of every supporting document.
C
DU is used exclusively for FHA loans while Loan Prospector (LP) is used for all conventional and government-backed loans. Human underwriters are only involved in manual underwriting scenarios when no AUS system is available.
D
Once DU issues an Approve/Eligible finding, lenders are no longer required to have a human underwriter review the file — the AUS finding alone is sufficient for loan funding under Fannie Mae guidelines.

3. A borrower receives a conditional approval with 12 conditions. Three of the conditions require the borrower to provide an updated pay stub, explain a $15,000 deposit in their bank account, and obtain a gift letter from their parents for the down payment. What is a conditional approval — and is the borrower approved for the loan?

A
A conditional approval means the loan has been denied subject to the borrower meeting conditions — the borrower is not approved until all 12 conditions are cleared and a second full underwriting review is completed.
B
A conditional approval means the underwriter has approved the loan subject to the listed conditions being satisfied. The borrower IS approved — but the loan cannot fund until all conditions are cleared. The three borrower conditions described are routine: an updated pay stub confirms continued employment, the deposit explanation rules out undisclosed debt, and the gift letter documents an acceptable source of funds. Once all conditions are cleared, the underwriter issues a clear to close and the closing disclosure can be sent.
C
A conditional approval with 12 conditions is effectively a denial — lenders who intend to approve loans issue clean approvals with no conditions. A high number of conditions indicates the loan does not meet program guidelines.
D
A conditional approval is the same as a pre-approval — it gives the borrower permission to shop for a home but does not commit the lender to funding the loan regardless of conditions.

4. A borrower is refinancing their primary residence. They sign all the loan documents at the closing table on Monday. When is the earliest the lender can disburse funds — and why?

A
Immediately — once the borrower signs the closing documents, the lender can disburse funds the same day. There is no waiting period for refinance transactions.
B
Tuesday — funds can be disbursed the next business day after all closing documents are signed, which is standard practice for both purchase and refinance transactions.
C
Thursday at the earliest. Under TILA's right of rescission, borrowers have 3 business days to cancel a refinance on their primary residence after signing. Business days exclude Sundays and federal holidays. Signing on Monday: Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday count as the three business days. The right of rescission expires at midnight Thursday, making Friday the first day funds can technically be disbursed — though Thursday is the earliest if disbursement happens after midnight. This right of rescission applies to refinances, HELOCs, and home equity loans — NOT purchase transactions.
D
The right of rescission does not apply to refinances — it only applies to purchase transactions where the borrower is buying a property they have not previously owned.

5. Three days before a scheduled closing, the loan officer learns that the borrower was laid off from their job two days ago. The closing disclosure has already been sent. What is the correct course of action — and what law is most relevant?

A
Proceed with the closing as scheduled — the loan was approved based on the borrower's employment at the time of underwriting, and the lender is legally committed to fund the loan once the closing disclosure has been delivered.
B
Proceed only if the borrower has sufficient reserves to cover at least 6 months of mortgage payments — this compensating factor is sufficient to replace the employment income under most loan program guidelines.
C
Do not close without notifying the lender immediately. The borrower's loss of employment is a material change in their financial circumstances that directly affects their ability to repay the loan — which is the core requirement of the Ability to Repay (ATR) rule under TILA and qualified mortgage standards. Proceeding to close a loan knowing the borrower no longer qualifies is both an ethical violation and a potential legal violation. The lender must be notified, the file must be re-evaluated, and if the borrower no longer qualifies, the loan cannot fund.
D
The loan officer should advise the borrower not to mention the job loss to the lender — since the closing disclosure has already been issued, the lender is contractually obligated to fund the loan regardless of changed circumstances.
