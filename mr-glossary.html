A
Anode RodWater Heater MaintenanceModule 3
A magnesium or aluminum rod inside a water heater tank that corrodes preferentially, protecting the steel tank from rusting — a process called sacrificial corrosion. Should be inspected every 3 years and replaced when mostly consumed. Extending anode rod maintenance can add years to a tank's life and avoid premature water heater replacement.
💡 One of the most overlooked preventive maintenance items in apartment maintenance — and one of the highest-value 20-minute repairs a tech can do proactively.
B
Ball ValvePlumbing ComponentModule 3
A shutoff valve with a lever handle that rotates 90 degrees (quarter-turn) between fully open and fully closed. Far more reliable than older multi-turn oval-handle valves — easy to operate in an emergency and does not seize up with age. The preferred replacement when upgrading aging shutoff valves.
C
CAMTCertified Apartment Maintenance TechnicianNAA · ANSI AccreditedModules 1, 7, 8
The primary professional certification for apartment maintenance technicians in the United States, administered by the National Apartment Association (NAA). Requires 12 months of apartment or rental housing maintenance experience (concurrent with coursework), completion of five technical courses plus a soft skills course, and a passing score on the comprehensive exam. Cost: approximately $881 for NAA members / $1,101 for non-members. Adds $3–$6/hour to pay. Find local chapters at naahq.org.
💡 Some employers cover or reimburse CAMT exam costs upon completion — ask during the hiring conversation. The certification pays for itself within two months of the pay increase it generates.
CapacitorHVAC ComponentModule 4
An electrical component in HVAC outdoor units that stores and releases energy to help start and run the compressor and outdoor fan motor. A failed capacitor — often visually identifiable by a bulged or mushroom-topped case — is one of the most common causes of AC not cooling. Capacitor work requires knowledge of high-voltage discharge procedures and EPA 608 certification. Diagnosable with a multimeter that reads microfarads.
💡 A multimeter that reads microfarads (MFD) can test a capacitor without replacing it — valuable diagnostic skill once you have EPA 608 certification.
CartridgeFaucet ComponentModule 3
The internal valve component in modern cartridge-style faucets that controls water flow and temperature mixing. When a cartridge faucet drips, the cartridge is usually worn and needs replacement. Cartridges are brand-specific — identify the faucet brand and model number before ordering a replacement. Cost: $15–$30 for most major brands.
Channel LocksHand Tool · EssentialModule 2
Adjustable pliers with a slip-joint mechanism allowing the jaw to be set at multiple widths. One of the most-used tools in apartment maintenance — for gripping pipes, fixtures, supply lines, and hundreds of other applications. Get at least two sizes: 10" and 6". Channel Lock brand is recommended for quality at a reasonable price.
Condensate Drain LineHVAC MaintenanceModule 4
The pipe that drains condensation from the AC evaporator coil's drain pan. When clogged, water backs up in the drain pan and triggers the float switch safety shutoff, cutting power to the unit. Clear by flushing with a 50/50 bleach and water solution or using a wet-vac at the outdoor drain termination. Flush the condensate line at the start of every cooling season — standard preventive maintenance.
Condenser CoilsRefrigerator MaintenanceModule 5
The heat-releasing coils on a refrigerator, located underneath behind the kickplate or on the back of the unit. Over time they collect dust and debris that insulates them, forcing the compressor to work harder. Clean annually with a coil brush and vacuum. Dirty coils are the most common and most overlooked cause of refrigerators running warm or cycling excessively — a 10-minute cleaning often fixes a "refrigerator not cooling" work order before any parts are ordered.
💡 Clean the coils before calling for service or ordering parts. Nine times out of ten, it resolves the complaint.
CPOCertified Pool/Spa OperatorPHTA CertificationModules 1, 8
Professional certification covering pool and spa water chemistry, equipment operation, maintenance procedures, and health and safety regulations. Required at most apartment communities with pools. Two-day course plus exam. Cost: $250–$400. Valid for 5 years, renewable. Find courses at phta.org or pooloperator.com. Pay impact: +$1–$3/hour or explicit per-property management premium.
Cutting InPainting TechniqueModule 6
The process of painting a precise strip along edges — where walls meet ceilings, at corners, around trim and fixtures — using an angled brush before rolling the main wall area. Cut in one wall section at a time and roll it immediately while the cut edge is still wet to prevent visible lap marks at the boundary. The roller cannot reach close enough to edges cleanly — cutting in first is non-negotiable for a professional finish.
D
Dip TubeWater Heater ComponentModule 3
A tube inside the water heater tank that carries incoming cold water to the bottom of the tank, keeping it separated from the hot water at the top. When the dip tube cracks or breaks, cold water mixes with the hot water at the top and residents receive lukewarm water even though the heater is functioning. Replacement is a standard maintenance tech repair.
Drain SnakeHand Auger / Closet AugerModule 3
A flexible cable tool used to break up or remove clogs deeper in drain pipes than a plunger can reach. Available in hand (manual) and electric versions. For sink drains, a basic hand snake reaches several feet into the drain line. For toilets, use a toilet auger (closet auger) specifically designed for toilet drain geometry — the vinyl sleeve protects the porcelain from scratching.
Dryer VentLife-Safety Maintenance ItemModule 5
The duct that exhausts hot, lint-laden air from a dryer to the outside of the building. When clogged with lint, it restricts airflow, causes dryers to overheat, blows thermal fuses repeatedly, and is a leading cause of residential fires. Signs of a clogged vent: dryer takes multiple cycles to dry, exterior vent flap does not open during operation, dryer feels very hot to the touch. Clean at least annually. Always clean the dryer vent before replacing a thermal fuse — a clogged vent will blow the new fuse immediately.
E
EPA Section 608Federal Refrigerant Certification · LifetimeModules 1, 4, 8
Federal certification required under Section 608 of the Clean Air Act for anyone who handles regulated refrigerants — adding refrigerant, recovering it, or servicing equipment in ways that could release refrigerant. Applies in all 50 states regardless of state licensing. Exam cost: approximately $20. Does not expire — lifetime certification. Available at most HVAC supply houses. Pay impact: +$3–$5/hour. The highest return-on-investment certification available to any maintenance tech.
💡 Type II certification (high-pressure systems) covers most residential and apartment HVAC equipment. Universal certification covers all types.
Error CodeAppliance DiagnosticsModules 2, 5
A specific code displayed by a modern appliance (as a number, letter combination, or flashing light pattern) that indicates a specific fault or problem. Always look up error codes on the manufacturer's website or YouTube before diagnosing — they point directly to the problem and save significant troubleshooting time. Never start diagnosing a modern appliance without first checking whether it is displaying an error code.
European Cup HingeCabinet HardwareModule 6
The concealed hinge standard in most modern kitchen and bathroom cabinets. Consists of a cup mounted inside the door and an arm mounted on the cabinet frame. Fully adjustable in three directions with a Phillips screwdriver: side-to-side (left/right alignment), in-and-out (gap between door and frame), and depth (door position relative to cabinet face). Most cabinet door alignment problems are resolved in three minutes by adjusting these screws — no replacement needed.
💡 Three minutes of hinge adjustment resolves most "cabinet door won't close" or "door hangs unevenly" work orders.
Evaporator CoilHVAC ComponentModule 4
The indoor coil of an AC system where refrigerant absorbs heat from the air passing over it, producing cooled air. When airflow is restricted (clogged filter, blocked return) or refrigerant charge is low, the coil can freeze solid. To thaw a frozen coil: set the system to FAN ONLY (not cooling) and run until the ice melts. Replace the filter and restart. If freezing recurs, refrigerant charge may be low — EPA 608 licensed technician required.
F
Faucet AeratorFaucet ComponentModule 3
A small screen device threaded onto the end of a faucet spout that mixes air with water to create a smooth flow and reduce water use. When clogged with mineral deposits, it causes reduced flow or spray irregularity — the most common cause of resident "low water pressure" complaints at individual faucets. Clean by soaking in white vinegar for 30 minutes or replacing entirely (cost: $3–$8).
FeatheringDrywall TechniqueModule 6
Spreading joint compound in progressively wider, thinner coats so the patch edges blend invisibly into the surrounding wall. The final coat should extend 8–12 inches beyond the hole in all directions, tapering to nothing at the edges. Without feathering, the patch boundary creates a visible ridge that shows through paint regardless of how well it is sanded. Each coat is applied wider than the previous one.
Fill ValveBallcockToilet ComponentModule 3
The valve mechanism that controls how water enters the toilet tank after flushing. A failing fill valve causes the toilet to run continuously after the tank should be full, or to refill too slowly. Replacement: shut off the supply valve, flush, disconnect the supply line, unscrew the fill valve locknut. Cost: $12–$20. Standard maintenance tech repair.
Flame SensorFurnace ComponentModule 4
A small metal rod inside a gas furnace that sits in the burner flame and generates a small electrical current when heated, signaling the control board that ignition was successful. Over time, oxidation builds up on the rod (visible as white coating) and blocks the electrical signal, causing the furnace to light briefly and immediately shut off. Fix: remove the sensor (one screw), lightly sand the rod with fine sandpaper, reinstall. Takes five minutes and costs nothing.
💡 Dirty flame sensor is the single most common furnace failure. Many "furnace not working" emergency calls are resolved by cleaning this one inexpensive rod.
FlapperToilet Component · Most Common PartModule 3
The rubber seal at the bottom of a toilet tank that lifts when the toilet is flushed and drops back to seal the tank as it refills. The most commonly replaced toilet part — a worn or warped flapper allows water to constantly trickle into the bowl, causing the toilet to "run." Cost: $5–$12. Match the brand and size to the existing flush valve seat.
💡 A running toilet from a worn flapper can waste hundreds of gallons per month — resolving it quickly protects the property owner from elevated water bills.
FlashingPaint / Drywall DefectModule 6
A visible sheen or color difference at a drywall patch after painting, caused by unprimed joint compound absorbing paint at a different rate than the surrounding primed wall. Even with a perfect color match, an unprimed patch will flash. Prevented entirely by applying drywall primer to any patched area before painting and allowing it to dry fully.
G
General Liability InsuranceIndependent Contractor EssentialModule 8
Insurance coverage that protects an independent contractor against claims of property damage and bodily injury that occur during their work. Standard minimum for maintenance contractors is $1 million per occurrence. Most property management companies require a certificate of insurance before awarding any contract work — you cannot get the contract without it. Cost: $500–$1,500/year. Must be obtained before the first paid independent job.
GFCIGround Fault Circuit InterrupterElectrical Safety DeviceModules 2, 4
A shock protection device built into outlets near water sources (kitchens, bathrooms, garages, exteriors) that cuts power instantly when it detects a current imbalance. One GFCI outlet often protects multiple downstream outlets on the same circuit — which is why dead outlets in one room are often caused by a tripped GFCI in a completely different room. To reset: press the RESET button on the GFCI outlet. If it trips again, investigate the circuit for a fault before resetting.
Graphite LubricantLock MaintenanceModule 6
A dry lubricant made from powdered graphite, applied via squeeze tube directly into key lock keyholes. The correct lubricant for any pin-tumbler lock — does not attract dirt or gum up with age. Never use WD-40 or oil-based products in key locks — they attract debris and progressively stiffen the lock over time. In a pinch, rubbing a pencil on the key inserts graphite the same way.
💡 Never use WD-40 in a key lock. It is oil-based and will make the lock worse over time.
H
Heat ExchangerFurnace Component · Licensed HVAC OnlyModule 4
The metal component inside a gas furnace that separates combustion gases from the air being distributed through the building. When cracked, combustion gases including carbon monoxide can enter the air distribution system. Visual sign: orange or yellow flames instead of blue during operation. A cracked heat exchanger is a life-safety issue requiring immediate furnace shutdown and licensed HVAC contractor inspection — never a maintenance tech repair.
High-Value SwapProfessional ConceptModules 2, 5
A repair that appears complex or specialized from the outside but is actually a simple part replacement requiring no license and minimal tools. Examples: oven igniter ($20–$50 part, 20 minutes vs. $150–$300 service call), refrigerator door gasket ($20, 10 minutes), dryer heating element ($30, 30 minutes). Knowing which repairs are high-value swaps is a core competency that makes a maintenance tech extremely valuable to property managers.
J
Joint CompoundDrywall MudDrywall MaterialModule 6
A gypsum-based paste used to finish drywall seams and patches. Dries by water evaporation — thick coats crack as they dry unevenly. Always apply in three thin coats, each dried completely before the next. Sand between coats (120-grit) and final-sand with 220-grit. Always prime joint compound before painting to prevent flashing. Setting-type compound dries faster via chemical reaction and does not shrink — useful when time is limited.
L
LLCLimited Liability CompanyBusiness StructureModule 8
A business structure that separates personal assets from business liability. If a client sues an LLC for a repair that went wrong, the owner's personal savings, home, and vehicle are protected. Operating as a sole proprietor without an LLC leaves personal assets exposed to business liability. Formation: $50–$500 depending on state, filed online through the state's Secretary of State website. An EIN (free at irs.gov) is needed for a business bank account and tax filing.
M
Maintenance ManagerSenior RoleModule 1
Senior maintenance role responsible for planning, scheduling, budgeting, vendor management, and team leadership across a property or portfolio. At smaller properties, the manager often completes repairs themselves. At larger properties (200+ units), this is a pure leadership position. Salary range: $55,000–$100,000+ per year with full benefits.
Maintenance SupervisorMid-Level Leadership RoleModules 1, 7
Mid-level maintenance role found at larger properties with multiple technicians on staff. Organizes daily tasks, ensures compliance with safety standards, oversees technicians, and coordinates with property management on scheduling and priorities. Typically requires 3–5 years of experience. Often includes on-site housing benefit. Salary range: $35,000–$55,000/year.
Maintenance TechnicianPrimary Front-Line RoleModule 1
The primary front-line role in apartment maintenance. Handles the bulk of day-to-day repair and upkeep tasks — responding to work orders, performing routine preventive maintenance, and handling emergency repairs. Requires a broad skill set across plumbing, electrical, HVAC, appliances, and interior repairs. Where almost everyone in apartment maintenance starts their career after the porter level.
Make-Ready TechnicianProperty Management RoleModules 6, 7
A maintenance tech role focused specifically on preparing apartment units for new residents — patching, painting, cleaning, replacing hardware, and completing the full turn-ready checklist between tenants. Common job title for entry to mid-level maintenance positions. Some properties have dedicated make-ready teams; others rotate the work across all maintenance staff.
MERV RatingMinimum Efficiency Reporting ValueFilter RatingModule 4
A scale from 1–16 rating an air filter's ability to capture airborne particles. Higher MERV = finer filtration but also greater airflow restriction. For standard 1-inch filter slots, MERV 8 is recommended. MERV 11–13 filters in 1-inch slots restrict airflow enough to damage the system over time. If high-efficiency filtration is needed, a dedicated 4-inch filter cabinet is required.
Model NumberRepair ResearchModules 2, 5
A unique identifier for a specific appliance or equipment unit, usually found on a label inside the door, on the back panel, or underneath the unit. The most important piece of information to collect before researching any unfamiliar repair. A model-specific YouTube search returns exact repair videos for that exact unit — far more useful than generic searches by symptom alone.
MultimeterElectrical Diagnostic ToolModules 2, 4
A handheld electronic measuring instrument used to test voltage, continuity, resistance (ohms), and — in models that support it — capacitance (microfarads). Essential for diagnosing electrical faults and testing HVAC capacitors. Get one that reads microfarads — it unlocks HVAC diagnostic work, which is the highest-paid skill set in apartment maintenance.
💡 If you have a multimeter and want to learn HVAC diagnostics, supervisors are much more likely to teach you — it signals seriousness and real ambition.
N
NAANational Apartment AssociationIndustry OrganizationModules 7, 8
The largest apartment industry organization in the United States, representing over 10 million apartment homes and 145 state and local affiliates. Administers the CAMT certification and other apartment industry credentials. Find your local affiliate at naahq.org to begin CAMT enrollment.
Non-Contact Voltage TesterElectrical Safety Tool · Must-HaveModules 2, 4
A pen-shaped tool that detects the presence of electrical voltage without touching the wire — hold it near any wire and it beeps or flashes if voltage is present. Required before touching any wire in any electrical repair, even after turning off a breaker. Breakers are frequently mislabeled in older buildings. Always test. Cost: $15–$25.
O
On-Call RotationMaintenance Employment ConditionModule 7
A schedule requirement at most apartment properties where maintenance techs take turns carrying an emergency phone and responding to after-hours calls — typically one week at a time, rotating among staff. Emergency calls usually involve water leaks, heating/cooling failures, lockouts, and safety concerns. Most calls are resolved in under an hour. Ask about on-call structure before accepting any maintenance position.
On-Site Housing BenefitProperty Management CompensationModule 7
Free or reduced-rate apartment housing provided to maintenance supervisors and senior techs as part of their compensation package. A $1,300/month housing benefit adds $15,600/year in real compensation value. Always calculate total compensation including housing when comparing property management offers — it dramatically changes the financial picture at the supervisor level.
OSHA 10 / OSHA 30Safety Certification · DOLModules 1, 8
Safety awareness certifications from the Occupational Safety and Health Administration. OSHA 10 is a 10-hour course covering basic workplace safety — widely recognized and increasingly required by large property management operators. OSHA 30 is a 30-hour course preferred for supervisory roles. Available online and in-person. Cost: $30–$100. Pay impact: +$0.50–$2/hour.
P
Paint SheenPaint SelectionModule 6
The level of reflectivity in a dried paint finish. Flat: ceilings only — hides imperfections, cannot be cleaned. Eggshell: lightly wipeable — bedrooms and low-traffic walls. Satin: moderate sheen, cleanable, durable — the standard for most apartment walls. Semi-gloss: high sheen, very cleanable, moisture-resistant — trim, doors, bathrooms, and kitchens. Never use flat paint in bathrooms or kitchens.
P-TrapDrain ComponentModule 3
The U-shaped curved section of drain pipe directly under a sink or other plumbing fixture. Holds a small amount of water at all times to create a seal that prevents sewer gases from entering the unit. Collects hair, soap scum, and debris — cleaning the P-trap resolves the majority of slow sink drain complaints. Always place a bucket underneath before removing the P-trap.
Porter / GroundskeeperEntry-Level RoleModules 1, 7
The standard entry-level position in apartment maintenance. Responsibilities include grounds cleanup, trash removal, common area cleaning, and painting apartments. No prior experience required. This is how most maintenance techs get their foot in the door — learn the property, demonstrate reliability, and work up to technician responsibilities within 6–18 months. Pay range: $15–$19/hour.
PPEPersonal Protective EquipmentModule 2
Protective gear worn to reduce exposure to workplace hazards. For maintenance techs: safety glasses (drilling, cutting, under-sink work), work gloves (cut-resistant and nitrile disposable), steel-toe boots (daily wear), dust mask/N95 (drywall, insulation, attic work), knee pads (floor-level plumbing work). PPE should be standard practice, not an afterthought.
S
Self-Employment TaxIndependent Contractor TaxModule 8
A 15.3% federal tax that independent contractors pay on net self-employment income, covering both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare. Employees only pay 7.65% because their employer covers the other half. This is why independent contractors must charge 1.5–2x their target net hourly rate. Set aside 25–30% of every payment for quarterly estimated taxes to avoid IRS penalties.
Strike PlateDoor HardwareModule 6
The metal plate mortised into the door frame that the latch bolt engages when the door closes. When misaligned, the door does not latch without force. Fix: loosen the mounting screws and shift the plate in the needed direction, or file the opening to enlarge it slightly. The latch bolt should engage smoothly with no resistance.
T
Teflon TapeThread Seal Tape / PTFE TapePlumbing SupplyModules 2, 3
A thin white tape wrapped around threaded pipe fittings before assembly to create a watertight seal. Required on virtually every plumbing repair involving threaded connections — supply line connections, shower arms, toilet supply lines, and more. Wrap clockwise (same direction as tightening) for proper sealing. Inexpensive and essential — keep it in your bag at all times.
Temp-to-HireEmployment PathwayModule 7
An employment arrangement where a staffing agency places a worker at a property management company on a temporary basis, with the expectation of conversion to permanent employment if the placement is successful. Common in property management maintenance hiring. Lower initial pay than direct hire, but provides a pathway to employment without requiring the full hiring commitment upfront. Companies like TrueBlue specialize in this model.
T&P ValveTemperature & Pressure Relief ValveSafety DeviceModule 3
A critical safety valve on every water heater that opens automatically if the tank pressure or temperature exceeds safe levels, preventing catastrophic tank failure. Should never be capped or disabled. A T&P valve that drips continuously indicates excess tank pressure — this requires professional diagnosis, not maintenance tech repair. Test annually by briefly lifting the lever (have a bucket ready).
Thermal FuseDryer Safety ComponentModule 5
A one-time safety device in electric dryers that opens permanently if the dryer overheats, cutting power to the heating element. A blown thermal fuse is one of the most common causes of a dryer that runs but does not heat. Test with a multimeter for continuity. Cost: approximately $10. Always clean the dryer vent before replacing the fuse — a clogged vent causes overheating and will blow the new fuse immediately.
ThermocoupleGas Water Heater ComponentModule 3
A small metal probe that sits in the pilot flame of a gas water heater and generates a small electrical current when heated. This current signals the gas valve to remain open. When the thermocouple fails, the pilot lights but goes out immediately when the button is released — the classic symptom. Replacement cost: $10–$20. Standard maintenance tech repair.
Thermostat Wiring TerminalsHVAC Low-Voltage WiringModule 4
The labeled terminals on any thermostat that connect to the HVAC system via low-voltage wires. Standard labels: R = 24-volt power (red wire), G = fan (green wire), Y = cooling/compressor (yellow wire), W = heat (white wire), C = common wire for digital thermostats. Always photograph the existing wiring before removing any thermostat — labels on wires may not match standard colors if previous techs swapped wires.
💡 Photo the wiring first, always. One missed wire connection on a thermostat swap can prevent heating or cooling from working.
Thermometer (Digital)Diagnostic Tool · DocumentationModules 2, 4
A digital temperature measuring device used to document actual temperatures rather than estimates. Used to measure AC vent output temperature, hot water temperature at the tap, and other temperature-dependent diagnostics. Eliminates "your word vs. the resident's word" disputes by producing objective, documentable data. Cost: $15–$30. One of the most underrated tools in apartment maintenance.
Turn-ReadyMake-ReadyProperty Management StandardModules 6, 7
The condition of an apartment unit fully prepared for a new resident to move in. Every door, window, electrical outlet, light, plumbing fixture, appliance, wall surface, floor, and cabinet confirmed in proper working condition. Lockset re-keyed. Caulk inspected. HVAC filter replaced. A unit that is fully turn-ready does not generate maintenance calls in the first two weeks of occupancy — the standard every maintenance tech is held to at turnover.
U
Unit ScanProfessional Maintenance HabitModules 1, 7
A 60-second visual sweep performed by a maintenance tech before leaving any unit after completing a work order. Checks six key areas: under the kitchen sink, toilet base, water heater area, HVAC filter, caulking around tubs and sinks, and smoke detector. Catches unreported problems early — before they become expensive damage. Should be documented in work order notes every time it is performed.
💡 One spotted slow leak can save a property owner $4,000+ in water damage repairs. The Unit Scan is the habit that earns trust and builds careers.
V
Vinyl Floor BubbleFlooring DefectModule 6
An area of vinyl flooring that has separated from the subfloor and lifted, creating a raised area. Almost always caused by moisture underneath — from a plumbing leak, condensation, or subfloor moisture. Must not be re-glued until the moisture source is identified and eliminated. Fix the moisture source, allow the subfloor to dry completely, then re-adhere and weight the flooring for 24 hours.
W
Wax RingToilet Installation ComponentModule 3
A soft wax seal between the base of a toilet and the drain flange in the floor that creates a watertight connection. Signs of a failed wax ring: water pooling around the base of the toilet, or a toilet that rocks. Replacement requires removing the toilet, scraping off the old ring completely, installing a new ring, and carefully lowering the toilet back onto the floor flange. Cost: $8–$15.
WeatherstrippingWindow / Door MaintenanceModule 6
Flexible sealing material along window and door frames that blocks drafts and moisture when the window or door is closed. Test: close on a dollar bill — if it slides out freely, the weatherstripping has compressed and needs replacement. Self-adhesive foam tape is the standard replacement for windows — peel off old material completely, clean the surface, apply new tape. Cost: $5–$10 per window.
Work OrderProperty Management System · Documentation StandardModules 1, 7
A documented repair or maintenance request submitted through the property management system that authorizes the maintenance tech to enter a specific unit for a specific task. Also the written record of what was found, what was done, what parts were used, and what was observed during the visit. Never enter a unit or perform repairs without a work order — it protects the tech, the resident, and the property. Write what you found and what you did, not just "fixed."
#
1099 ContractorEmployment ClassificationModule 8
An independent contractor who receives IRS Form 1099 (not W-2) for payment — meaning no taxes are withheld and the contractor is responsible for their own self-employment tax, estimated tax payments, and business expenses. The standard classification for independent maintenance contractors working with property management companies. 1099 work offers flexibility and higher gross rates but requires the contractor to manage their own taxes, insurance, and benefits.
