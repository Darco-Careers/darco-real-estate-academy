Pre-Screening & Showing Units

The tour is where leases are won or lost. Before a prospect ever steps on the property, a great leasing consultant has already asked the right questions, confirmed the right details, and set the stage for a showing that feels less like a sales pitch and more like a welcome home.

Lesson 1

Why Pre-Screening Matters — Before the Tour Begins

A prospect who tours your community and falls in love with it — only to be declined during the application process — is a frustrating outcome for everyone. They wasted time. You wasted time. And a unit that could have been shown to a qualified prospect sat idle for another day or two.

Pre-screening is the conversation you have before the tour to make sure a prospect is likely to qualify for the unit. It is not gatekeeping — it is customer service. Knowing upfront that a prospect's budget, timeline, or situation doesn't fit your available units saves them from a disappointing experience and keeps your schedule clear for qualified prospects.

What Pre-Screening Accomplishes

For You

Protects Your Time

Tours take 30–60 minutes each. Pre-screening a prospect in 5 minutes by phone before booking ensures every tour you conduct has a genuine chance of converting to an application.

For the Prospect

Prevents Disappointment

A prospect who learns upfront that the rent is $500 above their budget doesn't spend their Saturday touring a unit they cannot afford. That's a better experience — even if it means redirecting them.

For the Property

Maintains Occupancy Momentum

Every day a unit sits vacant costs money. A showing slot filled by an unqualified prospect is a showing slot not available to someone who can actually sign a lease.

For Fair Housing

Creates a Consistent Process

When you ask the same qualifying questions of every prospect in the same order, you demonstrate a consistent, non-discriminatory process. Inconsistency creates liability.

💡 Pre-Screening vs. Discrimination

Pre-screening is about the unit and the lease requirements — not about the person. You are asking whether the prospect's needs match what you have available: the price, the availability date, the unit size, and the lease term. You are never screening based on who the person is. Every question you ask must be one you would ask every single prospect, every single time.

Lesson 2

What You Can — and Cannot — Ask

This is where Fair Housing knowledge protects you in a real, practical way. The pre-screening call is a conversation about logistics — not a background investigation. There are clear boundaries between questions that are appropriate and questions that cross the line.

Appropriate Pre-Screening Questions

✓ Allowed

Move-in timeline

"When are you looking to move in?" Confirms alignment with your available date. A prospect who needs housing immediately cannot wait for a unit available in 45 days.

✓ Allowed

Budget and rent range

"Our available units start at $2,200/month — does that fit your budget?" Applied consistently to every prospect. Not about income — about whether the rent is within range.

✓ Allowed

Number of occupants

"How many people will be living in the unit?" This determines whether the unit size is appropriate. You cannot ask about the relationship or ages of the occupants.

✓ Allowed

Pets

"Do you have any pets?" If the community has a no-pet policy or breed restrictions, this pre-qualifies the prospect before the tour. Be consistent — ask everyone.

✓ Allowed

Unit size preference

"Are you looking for a one-bedroom or two-bedroom?" Helps match the prospect to available inventory and avoids showing a unit that doesn't meet their needs.

✓ Allowed

Lease term

"We offer 12-month leases — does that work for your situation?" Month-to-month or short-term needs should be surfaced before the tour, not after.

Questions You Must Never Ask (Or Prematurely Force)

✗ Prohibited

Where are you from? / What country?

National origin is a protected class. This question — however casually phrased — has no place in a pre-screening call and creates direct Fair Housing liability.

✗ Prohibited

Are you married? / Do you have kids?

Familial status is protected. You cannot inquire about whether occupants are related, married, have children, or are expecting. Ask only how many people will occupy the unit.

✗ Prohibited

What religion do you practice?

Religion is a federal protected class. Any question that touches on religious affiliation, practice, or preference is a violation — including seemingly friendly conversation.

⚠️ Mismatched Workflow

"Where do you work? / What do you do?"

While stating gross income requirements (e.g., 3x rent) is fully lawful and standard, digging into employment details, job titles, or specific employers over a brief introductory call is an operational mismatch. Save formal employment and income verification checks for the application processing module.

✗ Prohibited

Do you have a disability?

Disability is protected. You cannot ask — ever. If a prospect volunteers information about a disability or requests an accommodation, your obligation is to listen and respond appropriately, not to ask questions about the nature of their condition.

✗ Prohibited

Do you receive housing assistance?

In many states and major municipalities, source of income — including Section 8 / Housing Choice Vouchers — is a protected class. Asking this question as a pre-screening filter is an immediate legal violation in these jurisdictions.

⚠️ The "Just Curious" Trap

Some Fair Housing violations happen not from policy but from casual small talk. "Where are you originally from?" "Oh, do you have kids?" — these can all feel like friendly conversation. In the context of a leasing call, they are not. Keep pre-screening focused on the unit requirements. Save the small talk for after the tour.

Lesson 3

Preparing for the Tour — The Pre-Showing Checklist

A great tour does not start when the prospect arrives. It starts 20–30 minutes before they walk through the door. Leasing consultants who treat every tour as a prepared, deliberate presentation — not a casual walk-through — consistently outperform those who wing it. The preparation is what makes the tour feel effortless.

The Pre-Tour Checklist

✓
Review the prospect's inquiry and pre-screening notes

What unit size are they looking for? What is their move-in date? Do they have pets? Knowing this before they arrive lets you personalize the tour instead of re-asking questions they already answered.

✓
Confirm the appointment 24 hours in advance

Send a quick text or email the day before: "Hi [Name] — confirming your tour tomorrow at 2pm. We're at [address]. Looking forward to showing you around!" This dramatically reduces no-shows and signals professionalism.

✓
Inspect and prep the unit being shown

If showing a specific available unit — not just the model — walk it in advance. Lights on, blinds open, surfaces clear, no odors, no maintenance issues visible. A unit that looks neglected signals to the prospect that the management is also neglected.

✓
Check the model unit

If you are using the model: fresh flowers or fruit on the counter, lights on throughout, scent neutral or subtly fresh, nothing out of place. The model is your showroom — treat it like one.

✓
Know the current pricing and availability

Check your property management software before the tour so you have current rent amounts, move-in specials, and available dates ready. Nothing undermines credibility faster than not knowing what your own units rent for.

D
Plan your tour route

Walk the prospect through the best parts of the community first — lobby, amenities, then the unit. End at the leasing office where you can sit down and talk about next steps. Never start at the dumpsters or the parking structure.

D
Have an application ready

Whether paper or digital, have the application process ready to explain — or begin — immediately after the tour ends. A qualified prospect who loved the unit and has to wait a day to figure out how to apply often loses momentum.

✅ The First 60 Seconds

When a prospect walks into your leasing office, the impression you make in the first 60 seconds sets the tone for the entire tour. Stand up to greet them. Use their name. Offer water. Say something that shows you remember them: "You mentioned you have a cat — I have a unit on the second floor with a private patio you're going to love." That one detail signals you were listening and makes the tour feel personal, not transactional.

Lesson 4

Conducting the Tour — The Leasing Consultant's Playbook

The tour is not a recitation of square footage and appliance brands. It is a conversation about how this place could become the prospect's home. The most effective leasing consultants guide the tour with a purpose — they know what to show, what to say, and how to read the prospect's reactions in real time.

The Tour Flow — Step by Step

1
Start at the leasing office — build rapport first

Offer a seat, offer water, and spend 2–3 minutes connecting before you leave for the tour. Ask open-ended questions: "What's bringing you to the area?" or "What's most important to you in your next place?" These answers guide everything you highlight on the tour.

2
Lead with the community's best amenities

Walk them through the lobby, gym, pool, rooftop, dog run — whatever your community's strongest selling points are. Prospects should feel the quality of the community before they see the unit. Sequence matters: first impression is everything.

3
Approach the unit from the best angle

If there is a scenic courtyard route or a hallway that shows off the building's quality, take it. Avoid service corridors, loading areas, or anything that undermines the impression you are building. Every step before the door opens is part of the sale.

4
Open the door — and let them walk in first

Step aside and let the prospect enter ahead of you. Give them a moment to take it in before you say anything. First reactions are honest — and the silence lets them form their own impression rather than reacting to your description of it.

5
Talk about lifestyle — not specs

"The kitchen gets great morning light" lands better than "the kitchen faces east." "You could set up a home office in this alcove" beats "the unit has 850 square feet." Connect the features to how the prospect will actually live there — especially using what they told you in step 1.

6
Watch for buying signals — and follow them

A prospect who opens closet doors, checks water pressure, or looks out the window is engaged. A prospect who checks their phone is not. If you see engagement, slow down and let them explore. If they seem disinterested, ask: "Is this the layout you had in mind, or would a different floor plan work better for you?"

7
End the tour back at the leasing office

Always return to your desk to close the conversation. Standing in the parking lot is not the right environment for discussing applications, pricing, or next steps. The office signals that a decision is appropriate now — and gives you access to everything you need to move the process forward.

R

"I always ask one question right after we walk in: 'Can you picture yourself living here?' It sounds simple but it's the most useful question in the tour. If they say yes, the rest of the conversation is about how to make it happen. If they hesitate, I know exactly what to address before we leave the unit. Don't be afraid to ask directly — most people appreciate it."

— Rafael, Senior Leasing Consultant — West Coast Portfolio

Lesson 5

Handling Objections & Moving to the Application

Most prospects who tour an apartment and don't apply the same day aren't saying no — they're saying not yet. An objection is almost always a question in disguise. Learning to hear what's actually behind "I want to think about it" or "It's a little above my budget" is what separates a leasing consultant who books tours from one who signs leases.

Common Objections and How to Handle Them

The Objection What It Usually Means How to Respond
"I want to think about it." They liked it but are not sure yet — or they are seeing other properties and don't want to commit before comparing. "Absolutely — what would you need to see or know to feel confident moving forward?" This surfaces the real concern without pressure.
"It's a little above my budget." They may be stretching, or they may be comparing to cheaper options that don't match the quality. Acknowledge it, then help them do the math. "Compared to a place at $1,900 with no parking, you're actually saving the cost of a parking space plus the stress of looking for street parking every night."
"I'm still looking at other places." They are comparison shopping — completely normal. They have not yet decided this is the right place. "That makes total sense. What's the most important thing you're weighing?" Then make the case for what your community does better than anything at the same price point.
"The unit feels a little small." They may genuinely need a larger unit — or they may not have been able to visualize how their furniture would fit. "Would it help to walk through the layout with your furniture in mind? A lot of people are surprised by how livable it feels once you place a couch and bed." If they need a larger unit, offer one.
"Can you hold it for me?" They want the unit but are not quite ready to apply today — possibly waiting on income, a roommate decision, or approval elsewhere. Be honest: "I can't hold it without a completed application and holding deposit — but I can tell you it's the only available unit in that tier right now, so if it feels right, starting the application today is the best way to secure it."

Asking for the Application

The application ask should feel like a natural next step — not a sales push. After the tour, once you are back at your desk, use a soft but direct close:

❌ Passive — Leaves It Vague
"So... let us know if you want to move forward! You can find the application on our website or just give us a call."
No urgency, no guidance, puts all momentum on the prospect. Most people will go home, life will happen, and they will never apply.
✓ Direct — Moves the Process Forward
"Based on everything you've told me, I think this unit is a strong fit. The application takes about 15 minutes — would you like to start it now while you're here, or would tonight work better? I'll send you the link either way."
Gives them two real options, both of which move forward. Not pushy — just clear. Most prospects who are ready will choose one of the two.
📋 If They're Not Ready Today

Not every tour ends in an application — and that is fine. What matters is that you leave the door open professionally. Before the prospect leaves, make sure you have their contact information in your system, give them your direct number or email, and set a specific follow-up: "I'll check in with you Thursday — by then I should also know if another unit on a higher floor is opening up." A specific follow-up beats a vague "feel free to reach out."

Module 4 — Key Takeaways

✓

Pre-screening is a customer service tool, not a filter. It ensures that the prospects you tour are genuinely matched to what you have available — saving time for everyone and protecting your schedule for qualified leads.

✓

Allowable pre-screening questions cover unit logistics only: move-in timeline, budget relative to available rent, number of occupants, pet ownership, unit size preference, and lease term. Nothing about the person's identity, background, or family situation.

✓

Tour preparation is not optional — it is the work that makes the tour look effortless. Confirm appointments, inspect units, know your pricing, and plan your route before every showing.

✓

Lead the tour with amenities, let the prospect walk into the unit first, talk about lifestyle rather than specs, and always end back at the leasing office where the application conversation belongs.

✓

Objections are usually questions in disguise. Ask what's behind the hesitation before responding. "What would you need to feel confident moving forward?" surfaces the real concern more reliably than addressing the stated objection directly.

✓

Always ask for the application directly and specifically. Offer two concrete options — start now or tonight — rather than leaving the next step vague. If they are not ready, set a specific follow-up time before they leave.
