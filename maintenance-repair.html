The Opportunity

Why trades are one of the best real estate careers nobody talks about

Real estate is physical. Every building eventually needs a leaking pipe fixed, an HVAC system replaced, a roof repaired, an electrical panel upgraded, or a full renovation. The people who do this work are essential to the entire real estate ecosystem — and they are in chronic short supply.

Skilled tradespeople who understand the real estate world — who speak the language of investors, property managers, and developers — are not just maintenance workers. They become trusted partners who get called first, paid well, and referred constantly. The combination of a trade skill and real estate knowledge is genuinely powerful.

"The most successful real estate investors all say the same thing: finding a reliable, honest, skilled contractor is harder than finding a good deal. If you can be that person — the one who shows up, does quality work, and communicates clearly — you will never be out of work."

Real World

A day in the life of a real estate maintenance professional

Whether you're a solo handyman serving a portfolio of landlords or running a contracting crew on a full renovation, your days are active, tangible, and financially rewarding when you run the business right.

7am

Early start

Load the van, check today's job schedule. Three jobs booked — a leaking faucet at a rental property, drywall repair in a vacant unit, and an estimate for a full kitchen rehab for an investor client.

8am

Urgent repair

Property manager called — leaking pipe in a tenant's unit. Diagnose, fix, document with photos. Responsive, reliable service is what keeps property managers calling you back over anyone else.

10am

Vacant unit turnaround

Drywall damage in an empty unit — patched and painted before the new tenant moves in Friday. Turnaround speed matters to every landlord with a vacant unit losing rent daily.

1pm

Investor walkthrough

Walk a newly acquired 8-unit with an investor client. Assess every unit, note deferred maintenance, estimate costs. Your detailed scope of work gives the investor confidence to move forward.

3pm

Materials & scheduling

Pick up materials for tomorrow. Return calls, confirm appointments, update your job management app. Running a trades business is 70% skill and 30% organization and communication.

5pm

Invoice and follow up

Send invoices for today's completed work. Follow up on last week's estimate — the investor is ready to start. Schedule the crew. The business grows one well-executed job at a time.

What You'll Need

Skills that build a thriving trades business

Essential

Technical Trade Skills

Your hands-on skill is your product. Whether plumbing, electrical, HVAC, or carpentry — invest in training, apprenticeships, and certifications to level up continuously.

Essential

Reliability & Communication

The number one complaint about contractors is they don't show up and don't communicate. The tradesperson who shows up on time, does what they said, and calls proactively is worth their weight in gold to any investor.

Essential

Accurate Estimating

Walk a property, assess scope, and provide an accurate written estimate. Underbidding kills profit. Overbidding loses jobs. Accuracy wins long-term relationships with repeat clients.

Essential

Real Estate Vocabulary

Understanding ARV, NOI, value-add, and turnovers makes you a smarter partner and more trusted advisor. Investors and property managers refer tradespeople who speak their language.

Helpful

Business Management

Tools like Jobber or Housecall Pro turn a skilled tradesperson into a professional business — better clients, higher rates, fewer headaches, faster payments.

Helpful

Florida Licensing

Florida requires licenses for electrical, plumbing, and general contracting above certain thresholds. A license means higher rates and larger projects. Check the Florida DBPR for your trade's requirements.

Your Roadmap

How to build a trades career in real estate

1

Choose your trade and start learning

Pick the trade that best matches your existing skills and interests. YouTube is a powerful free training resource. For licensed trades, enroll in a community college program or apprenticeship — the Florida DBPR website lists all licensing requirements by trade.

2

Get your Florida contractor's license

Florida requires licenses for plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and general contracting above certain project values. A license dramatically expands your market and commands significantly higher rates. Check floridabuilding.org for your specific requirements.

3

Target property managers and investors directly

These are your best clients — recurring work, prompt payment, and referrals when you do good work. Introduce yourself at local real estate investor meetups. Drop off business cards at property management offices. Be the person they call first.

4

Learn real estate investor language

Complete the Darco Academy core modules — especially Modules 2 and 3. When you can talk to investors about NOI, value-add potential, and what repairs actually increase property value, you become a trusted advisor — not just a contractor.

5

Build professional business systems

Use Jobber or Housecall Pro to manage quotes, jobs, invoices, and scheduling. A professional system means faster payments, fewer miscommunications, and the ability to grow without dropping anything.

6

Consider the investor path

Many successful real estate investors started in the trades. Understanding construction costs and what work adds real value gives you an enormous advantage when evaluating investment deals. Your trade skill is a foundation for everything else in this curriculum.

Honest Assessment

Is a trades career in real estate right for you?

You'll thrive if you...

  • Enjoy hands-on, physical, tangible work
  • Like solving problems and seeing immediate results
  • Are reliable — you show up and finish what you start
  • Want to build a business with minimal startup capital
  • Like variety — no two jobs or properties are the same
  • Are interested in eventually owning investment properties
  • Want income that is largely recession-resistant

You may struggle if you...

  • Dislike physical, outdoor, or hands-on work
  • Are not willing to invest time learning a trade
  • Have poor follow-through or struggle with deadlines
  • Prefer purely office-based or intellectual work
  • Are not comfortable with urgent, unexpected problems
  • Don't want to manage client relationships

The Language

Key terms every trades professional in real estate must know

Scope of Work

A detailed written description of exactly what repairs will be completed, materials used, and price. Protects you and your client from misunderstandings and scope creep.

Deferred Maintenance

Repairs postponed over time — leaking roofs, aging HVAC, old plumbing. Investors buying value-add properties specifically look for these and budget for fixing them.

CapEx — Capital Expenditure

Major long-term improvements — new roof, HVAC replacement, foundation work. Different from routine maintenance and tracked separately by property investors.

Turnover

Preparing a vacant rental unit for a new tenant — cleaning, painting, repairs, carpet replacement. Fast, quality turnovers are extremely valuable to property managers.

Punch List

A final list of small outstanding items to complete before a project is done. Knocking out the punch list promptly is what gets you final payment and repeat business.

Permit

Government approval required for certain types of work. Licensed contractors pull their own permits. Unpermitted work can create major legal issues for property owners at resale.

Value-Add

Renovations that directly increase a property's market value or rental income. Investors prioritize these over purely cosmetic repairs when budgeting a rehab.

Lien

A legal claim against a property for unpaid work. Contractors who aren't paid can file a mechanic's lien in Florida. Know your lien rights — they protect you.

GC — General Contractor

The contractor who manages the overall project — hiring and coordinating subcontractors, tracking the budget, and delivering the finished renovation on time.
