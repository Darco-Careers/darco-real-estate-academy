The Role

What does a residential agent actually do?

A residential real estate agent helps people buy and sell homes. You're the guide, the negotiator, the expert, and the trusted advisor all rolled into one. When someone decides to buy their first home or sell the house they raised their family in — you're the person they call.

As an agent, you earn a commission — typically 2.5–3% of the sale price — when a deal closes. On a $400,000 home that's $10,000–$12,000 from one transaction. On a $600,000 home in Miami, that's $15,000–$18,000. Every deal you close, every relationship you build, every referral you earn compounds into a real career and real income.

"Real estate is the only profession where you can start with little experience, build genuine relationships in your own community, and create a six-figure income within your first two years — if you're willing to put in the work."

The difference between an average agent and a great one isn't luck — it's knowledge, consistency, and people skills. The agents who invest in truly understanding the market, who communicate honestly with clients, and who build a reputation for integrity don't just survive in this business — they thrive.

The different roles in residential real estate

🤝

Buyer's Agent

Represents people who want to purchase a home. You help them find properties, analyze the market, make offers, and navigate the closing process.

📋

Listing Agent

Represents sellers. You advise on pricing, market the property, host showings, negotiate offers, and guide the seller to closing.

🏢

Real Estate Broker

A more advanced license that allows you to open your own brokerage, supervise agents, and keep 100% of your commissions rather than splitting with a broker.

🌟

Realtor

A licensed agent who is also a member of the National Association of Realtors (NAR). Subject to a code of ethics and gets access to the MLS and additional tools.

Getting Licensed

How to get your real estate license

Every state requires a real estate license to represent buyers and sellers — but the process follows the same general path nationwide. Requirements vary by state in terms of hours, fees, and exam format, but the six steps below apply universally. The entire process typically takes 2–4 months and costs $300–$800 depending on your state.

📋 How to Get Licensed — The Universal Path

No college degree required in any state. You need to be 18+ (19 in some states), have a Social Security number, and pass a background check. Everything else is learnable — and the process is designed to be completed while working another job.

01

Meet your state's basic requirements — typically 18+, a Social Security number, and a high school diploma or equivalent

02

Complete your state's required pre-license education — hours range from 40 to 180 depending on your state. Available online or in person through approved schools

03

Pass your state licensing exam — typically 100–150 multiple choice questions covering real estate law, finance, and practice. Most states require 70–75% to pass

04

Submit your application to your state's real estate regulatory commission — include your exam results, background check, and application fee

05

Complete a background check — criminal history does not automatically disqualify you but must be disclosed. Contact your state commission if you have questions

06

Join a licensed brokerage to activate your license — new agents must work under a supervising broker. You cannot practice independently on a salesperson license

To find the exact requirements for your state — hours, fees, exam format, and approved schools — visit your state's real estate regulatory commission website or Colibri Real Estate, the leading national licensing school operating in all 50 states.

Real World

A day in the life of a residential agent

No two days are exactly the same — which is part of what makes this career exciting. Here's what a productive day might look like for a new-to-mid-level agent building their business.

8am

Morning prospecting

Check new MLS listings in your target neighborhoods. Send follow-up messages to leads from last week. Call a past client to check in — referrals come from people who feel remembered and valued.

10am

Buyer consultation

Meet a first-time buyer couple at your office. Walk them through the buying process, explain financing, discuss their budget and must-haves. Build trust before you ever show them a single house.

12pm

Property showings

Drive clients through 3 homes in Coral Gables. Point out the good — and the not-so-good. Your honest assessment builds trust. Clients who trust you buy faster and refer their friends.

2pm

Listing appointment

Meet homeowners who want to sell. Present your comparative market analysis showing what similar homes sold for. Recommend a price. Walk through your marketing plan — photos, MLS, social media, open house.

4pm

Offer negotiation

Your buyers want to make an offer on a home listed at $485,000. You advise offering $475,000 with specific terms that make the offer stronger. Back and forth with the listing agent. This is where your market knowledge pays off.

6pm

Social media & marketing

Post a new listing to Instagram. Engage with comments. Share a market update. In today's real estate, your online presence is your reputation. Agents who market themselves consistently always have leads.

What You'll Need

Skills that make you a great agent

Essential

People Skills & Empathy

Buying a home is one of the most emotional experiences a person goes through. The ability to listen, understand, and communicate with warmth and patience separates great agents from average ones.

Essential

Market Knowledge

Know your local market cold — recent sales, price trends, neighborhood dynamics, school ratings, development plans. Clients pay you for knowledge. The more you know, the more valuable you are.

Essential

Negotiation

You negotiate on behalf of your clients on every single deal. Price, terms, repairs, closing costs, timelines. Great negotiators save (or make) their clients thousands of dollars per transaction.

Essential

Self-Motivation & Discipline

Nobody gives you leads. Nobody holds you accountable. Your income is directly tied to your effort. The agents who thrive treat real estate like a business — with systems, goals, and daily habits.

Helpful

Marketing & Social Media

Today's clients find their agents online. Agents who post market updates, property tours, and helpful content consistently build an audience that turns into referrals and listings.

Helpful

Financial Literacy

Understanding mortgages, financing options, and investment value makes you a much better advisor — especially when working with buyers who are also thinking about real estate as an investment.

Your Roadmap

How to launch your agent career

1

Complete your state's pre-license education

Hours vary by state — from 40 to 180. Take it online or in person through a state-approved school. Colibri Real Estate, Real Estate Express, and Kaplan operate nationally. Budget 4–8 weeks depending on your state's requirements and your pace. The course covers contracts, fair housing, financing, and your state's real estate law.

2

Pass your state licensing exam

Most state exams are 100–150 multiple-choice questions with a passing score of 70–75%. The most common reason people fail is not practicing enough. Use your state's approved prep materials and take as many practice tests as possible before your exam date.

3

Choose your brokerage carefully

Your first brokerage matters more than most new agents realize. Look for training programs, mentorship, a strong local reputation, and a commission split that makes sense for where you are. Keller Williams, Compass, eXp Realty, and RE/MAX all have strong national presences — but local boutique brokerages can be equally valuable depending on your market.

4

Build your sphere of influence from day one

Your database is your business. Tell everyone you know that you're now a licensed agent. Friends, family, neighbors, former colleagues. Most new agents' first 3–5 deals come directly from their personal network. Don't be shy about it.

5

Learn your market obsessively

Drive every neighborhood you want to work in. Study every listing that comes on the market and every sale that closes. Know the average price per square foot, the days on market, the seasonal trends. When clients ask questions, you need to have answers.

6

Show up consistently — especially when it's hard

Most new agents quit within 18 months because they expected quicker results. The agents who make it are the ones who keep prospecting, keep learning, and keep showing up even during slow stretches. Your pipeline today is your income 60–90 days from now.

Honest Assessment

Is this career right for you?

You'll thrive if you...

  • Genuinely enjoy meeting and helping people
  • Are self-motivated with no one managing your schedule
  • Can handle variable income and uncertainty
  • Are willing to prospect and market yourself consistently
  • Enjoy being out in the community and driving around
  • Are patient — first deals take time to materialize
  • Take pride in your local neighborhood knowledge

You may struggle if you...

  • Need a guaranteed salary from the start
  • Don't enjoy cold outreach or following up with people
  • Get discouraged easily by rejection
  • Prefer working alone behind a desk all day
  • Are uncomfortable with confrontation or negotiation
  • Don't want to work evenings and weekends occasionally

The Language

Key terms every agent must know

MLS — Multiple Listing Service

The database where all listed properties are shared among agents. Your access to the MLS is one of the most valuable things your license gives you.

CMA — Comparative Market Analysis

A report showing what similar homes sold for recently — used to help sellers price their home and buyers make competitive offers.

Commission

Your compensation — typically 2.5–3% of the sale price, paid at closing. Split with your broker according to your agreement.

Listing Agreement

A contract between you and a seller giving you the exclusive right to market and sell their property for a set period of time.

Buyer's Representation Agreement

A contract between you and a buyer establishing you as their exclusive agent and outlining how you'll be compensated.

Contingency

A condition in a purchase contract that must be met for the deal to proceed — financing contingency, inspection contingency, appraisal contingency.

Escrow

A neutral third party holds the buyer's deposit and all funds until closing conditions are met — protecting both buyer and seller.

Days on Market (DOM)

How long a property has been listed. A high DOM can indicate overpricing or problems. A low DOM suggests strong demand or a well-priced home.

Pre-Approval

A lender's written commitment to loan a buyer a certain amount. In competitive markets, sellers won't look at offers without one.
